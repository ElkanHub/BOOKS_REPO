# Chapter 10: Stop Outsourcing Your Thinking
### "You've been letting other people's conclusions crowd out your own. And you didn't even notice it happening."

---

Think about what made you angry this week.

Not what you saw that was genuinely wrong, harmful, or worth caring about. I'm asking what made you angry. What consumed your attention. What you talked about. What you posted about. What you argued about in group chats.

Now ask: why that thing? Why did that particular piece of content, that particular story, that particular outrage — why did it land in your feed and not something else? Why did it catch your attention and not the thousand other things happening in the world?

You didn't choose that anger. It was chosen for you. By an algorithm that studied what made you click, what made you pause, what made you react. The algorithm showed you what it knew would hold your attention. And you Mistook curated outrage for genuine thinking.

This is how thinking gets outsourced in the modern era. Not through some dramatic conspiracy. Through the slow, invisible narrowing of your information environment until you can't remember what it was like to have a thought that didn't come from a feed.

---

## How Algorithms Are Thinking for You

Every platform you use is designed to show you more of what you've already shown interest in.

This is presented as a feature. "Personalization." "Made for you." "Tailored to your interests." What it actually means is: the system has studied your patterns and will now feed you a version of reality that matches those patterns. It will confirm your existing beliefs. It will show you the people you agree with. It will make the world look like the version you've already decided is true.

You think you're forming opinions. You're actually just consuming content that aligns with opinions you already had. The feeling of validation — the righteous certainty you get when someone articulates exactly what you already thought — that's not thinking. That's reinforcement. That's your existing views being mirrored back at you through a screen.

The problem is that you don't notice the narrowing. The algorithm doesn't show you what's missing. You don't see the perspectives you're not seeing. You don't encounter the arguments that might challenge your thinking. You just keep seeing more of what you've already seen, and you think you're staying informed.

This is how smart people become intellectually lazy without realizing it. They consume a lot of content that confirms what they believe and call it being politically aware. They follow accounts that challenge easy narratives and feel like they're thinking critically when they're actually just following a different algorithm's recommendations. They mistake information consumption for intellectual work.

The algorithm is not your enemy. It's a tool. But a tool that shapes what you see shapes what you think about. And if you don't consciously curate your information environment, someone else will do it for you. The algorithm will do it for you. And the algorithm's goal is not your intellectual development. It's your engagement.

Engagement and thinking are not the same thing. Content that makes you angry is engaging. Content that challenges you is often boring. The algorithm will always prefer engagement over growth.

## The Group Think Tax

Here's a question most people never ask: do I actually believe this, or do I believe it because everyone I know believes it?

This is uncomfortable. It implies that your beliefs might not be fully yours. That the things you hold most strongly might be the things your crowd holds most strongly. That you've been absorbing other people's conclusions without examining them and calling the whole thing your own worldview.

Group think is invisible to the group. When everyone around you believes the same thing, that belief feels like fact. It feels like the obvious, rational, only-sensible conclusion. Anyone who disagrees must be missing something. They must be stupid, or naive, or malicious. Because the conclusion is so obviously correct to everyone you respect.

But groups are wrong all the time. Entire professions have believed things that turned out to be false. Entire cultures have held views that were clearly wrong by any ethical measure. The consensus was just consensus. It felt like truth because enough people agreed.

The cost of group think is not just that you hold false beliefs. It's that you stop knowing where your beliefs end and other people's begin. Your opinions and your crowd's opinions become the same thing. You can't tell anymore which thoughts are yours and which you've borrowed so thoroughly that they feel like yours.

This matters because the person who can't distinguish their own thinking from the crowd's thinking is not really a person with a worldview. They're a person with a community's worldview. And when circumstances change, when the community disperses, when you have to make a decision that nobody around you has made — you have nothing to stand on. You're just a person who agrees with people, and the people aren't there.

The group think tax is paid in quiet moments. In the decisions you can't make because you don't know what you actually think. In the times you go along with something you privately disagree with because disagreeing would be uncomfortable. In the years you spend thinking a certain way without ever having examined whether it holds up.

Over time, the tax compounds. You become a person who has opinions but not understanding. Who knows the conclusions but not the reasoning. Who can repeat what the smart people say but can't generate original thought of their own.

## How to Think From First Principles

First principles thinking is not complicated. It's just difficult to maintain.

The idea is simple: take a problem and break it down to its most basic, foundational elements. Then build your understanding from the ground up, rather than borrowing someone else's framework.

Here's how it works in practice. Someone says to you: you should go to graduate school. The conventional wisdom in your circle is that more education is better, that a master's degree opens doors, that it's worth the investment.

Instead of accepting this framework, you break it down. What is the actual claim being made? That graduate school leads to better career outcomes. Is that true? For what fields? For what people? Under what conditions? What would have to be true for this to be a good decision for you specifically?

You start questioning the assumptions. Is more education always better? No. Some fields don't care about degrees. Some careers are better advanced through experience. Some people would be better served by building something instead of studying it.

What are the actual outcomes you want? Better job? More money? Intellectual fulfillment? These are different things. Graduate school might serve one and not the others. If what you actually want is a better job, is there a more direct path?

This is first principles thinking. It's not rebellious for the sake of being rebellious. It's not rejecting conventional wisdom just because it's conventional. It's just: what actually is true? What actually works? What actually makes sense for my specific situation?

The alternative is to use someone else's framework. To let the default assumption — graduate school is good, the stable job is safe, marriage by 30 is the goal — run unexamined in your life. To make decisions based on what everyone around you has decided without ever asking whether those decisions fit you.

First principles thinking requires something most people don't have: the willingness to reach a conclusion that your community disagrees with. Because if you actually think through something from scratch, you might land somewhere that doesn't match what the people around you believe. And then you'll have to decide whether to follow the thinking or follow the crowd.

Most people don't. Most people think until they reach a conclusion their community would support, and then they stop. They call that thinking. It's actually just seeking approval through the medium of ideas.

## The Shift

Your mind is either your own or it belongs to whoever last influenced you.

This sounds harsh. But consider: every opinion you hold came from somewhere. From your parents. Your teachers. Your culture. Your algorithms. Your friends. The books you've read. The experiences you've had. At what point did you take all of that input and run it through your own thinking? At what point did you ask whether the conclusions you inherited are actually conclusions you would have reached on your own?

If the answer is never — if you've just been absorbing and repeating — then your mind is not really yours. It's a repository of other people's thoughts. It's well-stocked and reasonably organized, but it's not generating anything original.

The alternative is not to reject everything and start from scratch. You can't. You're a product of your context like everyone else. The alternative is to be more intentional about which thoughts you run with and which ones you hold loosely. To be the kind of person who can say: I was raised to believe this, my culture believes this, most people I know believe this — but I'm not sure what I actually think. And I'm willing to sit with that uncertainty instead of filling it with the first available answer.

Your mind is the one tool you cannot lose. Take care of whose conclusions you're putting into it.

## Close — The One Thing

Pick one belief you hold strongly — something political, something about how to live, something about what matters — and ask yourself: if nobody I knew held this belief, would I still believe it?

You don't have to change your mind. Just ask the question honestly. The answer might surprise you.
