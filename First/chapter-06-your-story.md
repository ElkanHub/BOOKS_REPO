# Chapter 6: The Story You Tell About Your Past Is Keeping You Stuck
### "You didn't just have a past. You adopted a story about it. That's the part that's keeping you stuck."

---

At some point in your life, something happened. Maybe it was small. Maybe it was significant. Maybe it was a series of things that accumulated over years. But something happened, and you drew a conclusion from it.

That conclusion became part of your story. And your story became part of you.

Maybe the conclusion was: I'm not the type of person who speaks up. Because once, in a meeting, you spoke up and someone shot your idea down in front of everyone. Now you stay quiet.

Maybe it was: I'm bad with money. Because you made a mistake when you were 21 and it took years to recover, and now every financial decision feels loaded with that original failure.

Maybe it was: people like me don't get easy. Because your family struggled and your neighborhood was difficult and you watched your parents work hard and still not get ahead, and somewhere in there you absorbed the belief that life is just hard for certain people and you might be one of them.

These are not facts. These are interpretations. But they've been repeated so many times — to yourself, to therapists, to friends who ask why you do what you do — that they feel like facts. They feel like the truth about who you are.

They're not. They're just the story you've been telling.

---

## How Your Story Got Written

Every identity is a narrative. You are not born with a fixed understanding of who you are. You construct it from the data of your experience, and then you select which data to emphasize, which to minimize, and which to ignore entirely.

This construction happens mostly before you're old enough to know it's happening.

When you were eight, your teacher told your parents you weren't very social. Your parents repeated this observation at dinner. You heard it. You internalized it. Now, twenty years later, you still describe yourself as introverted, as someone who doesn't warm up easily, as someone who takes time to trust. The original data point — one teacher's observation — has become a core feature of your identity.

When you were sixteen, you failed an exam badly. You remember your mother's face. You remember the silence in the car on the way home. You remember deciding that you weren't academic, that school wasn't your thing, that you needed to find another path. You might have been right about school. But you might have also been a sixteen-year-old who failed one exam and needed support and instead got a story that followed you for the rest of your education.

When you were twenty-two, a romantic relationship ended badly. You were the one who got left. You were the one who didn't see it coming. You rebuilt yourself afterward, but something stayed broken. Now you approach every relationship with a wall up. You've decided, based on one person's choice to leave, that people leave. That you're the kind of person who gets left.

None of these conclusions are crazy. They made sense at the time. They were attempts to make meaning out of painful experiences. But they've been running in the background of your life for years, shaping decisions you don't even realize you're making, closing doors you don't even realize you're walking past.

The worst part is that you've been reinforcing them. Every time you tell the story, the story gets more real. Every time you explain yourself using the story, you believe it more. You've become an expert in your own past, and the expertise keeps you exactly where you are.

---

## Why People Protect Their Pain

Here's something nobody tells you about pain: sometimes people don't want to let it go.

This is hard to hear. It sounds like I'm saying pain is a choice. It's not. Pain is real. What I'm saying is something more subtle: sometimes the story around the pain becomes useful. Sometimes holding onto the story gives you something, even if what it gives you is limited.

If your story is that the world is hard and you've had a rough go, then: not trying too hard makes sense. Playing it safe makes sense. Expecting disappointment makes sense. Limiting your ambitions makes sense. The story justifies the smallness. The smallness keeps you safe. And safe feels better than disappointed.

But safe is also where dreams go to die quietly.

I knew a man — let's call him Kofi — who had a rough childhood. He didn't have much. He worked hard. He built something for himself. By any external measure, he succeeded. But he was always waiting for it to fall apart. He could never enjoy the success because somewhere in his story was the belief that good things don't last for people like him. He'd sabotage things right before they got too good. He'd find reasons to doubt. He'd pull back right when something was working.

His pain had become his identity. And his identity was: the person things happen to. Not the person who makes things happen. The difference was invisible to him because he couldn't see his own patterns. To him, it just felt like the way life works.

He didn't want to let go of the story. Because the story was his armor. It protected him from the risk of hoping too much. It gave him an explanation for why things never quite worked out. It was uncomfortable, but it was familiar. And familiar, for most people, beats uncomfortable every time.

---

## The Difference Between Acknowledging Your Past and Being Owned By It

Your past happened. Some of it was not your fault. Some of it was genuinely hard. Some of it left marks.

You are allowed to say all of that. You should say all of that. Naming what happened to you is not weakness. It's honesty.

But there's a difference between acknowledging your past and being owned by it.

Being owned by your past means: I am this because of what happened to me. The thing that happened shaped me and now I am fixed. The story is the truth. The truth is the story. This is who I am and there's nothing to be done about it.

Acknowledging your past means: something happened. It affected me. It shaped certain choices I made and certain beliefs I formed. But I am not the same person I was at eight, or sixteen, or twenty-two. I am the person who has been living with these experiences, and I am also the person who gets to decide what they mean now.

You can honor your past without being imprisoned by it. You can carry the lessons without carrying the chains.

Here's what this looks like in practice: the person who was hurt by their family and now keeps everyone at a distance has to eventually ask whether the distance is protection or limitation. The person who failed at something once and decided they're bad at it has to eventually ask whether the original failure is still the defining fact or whether it's just one event in a longer story. The person who grew up with very little and now can't spend money without guilt has to eventually ask whether the scarcity story is still serving them or just keeping them small.

The past is not a prison. It's a chapter. You're the one holding the pen now.

That sentence sounds nice. But what does it actually mean?

It means you get to ask: is the story I'm telling about myself still true? Is it still useful? Is it still the best explanation for who I am and what I'm capable of? Or have I just been telling it for so long that I've forgotten it's a story and started treating it as a fact?

---

## The Shift

You are not your story. Your story is what happened to you. You are what you do with what happened to you.

This is not a call to ignore your past or pretend it didn't hurt. It's a call to stop letting it make decisions for you without your permission.

The next time you catch yourself saying "I'm the type of person who..." — pause. Ask: is this true? Did I choose to be this type of person, or did I just accept the story and stop looking for evidence otherwise?

You might find that the story you've been telling is not the only story. It's just the one you've been telling.

---

## Close — The One Thing

Think of one story you tell about yourself. Something you believe is true about who you are. Now ask: if I dropped this story — not because it's false, but because it might not be the whole truth — what would become possible?

You don't have to answer. Just sit with the question.
