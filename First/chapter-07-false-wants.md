# Chapter 7: You Don't Actually Want What You Think You Want
### "You didn't choose that goal. You inherited it. And when you got it, you expected to feel something that never came."

---

Abena is 29. She has a master's degree. She has a senior position at a reputable company. She has a boyfriend who is a good man. She has all the things she was supposed to want by this age.

She is not happy.

She thought she would be. She remembers being 22 and imagining what 29 would look like. This was the vision. This was the destination. And she arrived. She did everything right. She followed the map.

So why does she feel like she's living someone else's life? Why does she wake up some mornings and look in the mirror and not recognize herself? Why does she have everything she wanted and feel nothing like she expected?

Abena is not ungrateful. She's not broken. She's not suffering from some personal deficiency that therapy or medication or a vacation could fix.

Abena wanted the right things for the wrong reasons. And the wrong reasons hollow out even the right things until they feel like nothing at all.

---

## Borrowed Ambitions

At some point, probably without realizing it, you started wanting things that weren't yours.

Not things that were bad. Not things that were wrong. Just things that belonged to other people and somehow ended up in your want list.

Your father wanted you to be a lawyer. Not because he researched what would make you happy. Because his friend was a lawyer and it seemed like a good profession and he wanted you to have stability. So you started wanting to be a lawyer. Not consciously. It just became part of the air you breathed. The expectation was in the family. The expectation became your ambition.

Your mother wanted you married by 26. Not because she thought about what kind of partner would suit you. Because that's what women in her generation did and she wanted you to have someone to grow old with. So you started wanting marriage. You dated people who looked right on paper. You stayed in relationships past their expiration dates because the goal was marriage, not the specific person, and marriage required a body, any body, to aim at.

Your friends were all doing master's degrees. Not because they had a specific reason. Because that's what post-grad looked like in your circle. So you applied. You got in. You did it. It looked impressive on LinkedIn. It didn't change anything about how you felt about your work or your life.

Your community expected you to move back after school. Not because staying was impossible. Because leaving felt like rejection. So you came back. You told yourself it was practical. You told yourself you were being smart. You moved back into your parents' house and into the life you were supposed to want and you've been slowly disappearing ever since.

These are borrowed ambitions. They're not evil. They're not even wrong, necessarily. But they're not yours. They came from somewhere else, and they've been running your life like malware you didn't know was installed.

The test is simple: when you imagine getting the thing you say you want, how do you feel? If the answer is "relieved" rather than "excited," that's a warning sign. Relief is what you feel when an obligation ends. Excitement is what you feel when something you genuinely want is arriving.

If you're chasing something and all you feel is relief at the prospect of it being over, ask yourself whether you ever actually wanted it. Or whether you just wanted the story of having achieved it. The approval. The quiet nod from the people whose opinions you've been managing your whole life.

---

## The Test: Would You Still Want It If Nobody Knew?

Here's a question that cuts through borrowed desire like nothing else:

Would you want this if nobody could see it?

Would you want the career, the title, the house, the car, the relationship, the life — if you couldn't post about it? If nobody in your family knew about it? If it existed entirely in private and nobody would ever find out whether you had it or not?

If the answer changes when you strip away the audience, then the thing you want is not the thing. The thing you want is being seen as the person who has the thing. The recognition. The status. The proof that you succeeded by whatever metric your world is measuring.

This is not shallow. Status is real. Humans are wired for it. We care what our community thinks because our survival depended on our community for most of human history. Wanting recognition is not wrong. It's just not the same as wanting the thing itself.

But here's the problem: you have to live inside the thing. You don't have to live inside the status. Status is external. The thing is internal. The thing is what you'll be doing at 10 PM on a Tuesday when nobody's watching. The status is what you think about when someone asks how you're doing.

If you want something primarily for the status, you will eventually get tired of the performance. Because performing takes energy. And the thing itself — the actual career, the actual relationship, the actual life — will be hollow because it was designed for an audience, not for you.

Ask yourself: if I could have this in complete privacy, without anyone ever knowing, would I still pursue it? Would I still do the work, make the sacrifices, put in the hours?

If yes, the wanting is yours.

If no, you're chasing something for the wrong reasons. And no amount of achieving it will fill the space where genuine desire should be.

---

## Desire Under Pressure

Peer pressure doesn't end at 18. It just gets more sophisticated.

At 16, peer pressure looks like pressure to drink at a party, to try something stupid, to impress someone whose opinion doesn't matter past next week. At 26, peer pressure looks like pressure to have the career, the relationship, the apartment, the life. It looks like subtle judgment when someone hasn't done the expected things by the expected age. It looks like questions that aren't really questions — "So are you guys engaged yet?" "When are you going back to school?" "Aren't you worried about your biological clock?" — and the implied answer is always yes, you should be worried, you should be doing more, you should be further along.

This pressure goes underground. It disguises itself as ambition, practicality, and wisdom. It sounds like: "You need to be realistic." It sounds like: "The creative thing is risky, you should have a backup plan." It sounds like: "Everyone wants to do something meaningful, but bills don't pay themselves."

These voices are not always wrong. Sometimes bills don't pay themselves. Sometimes you do need a practical plan. But sometimes the voices are just the community protecting its own beliefs about how life should go. Sometimes the voices are your parents' fears dressed up as advice. Sometimes the voices are your own fear of wanting something that might not work out, looking for external validation to justify the risk.

Desire under pressure is not really desire. It's compromise that hasn't admitted it's compromise. It's the version of your life that causes the least friction with everyone around you. It's the life that fits perfectly into the expectations of the people who already know you.

There's nothing wrong with choosing a practical path. Sometimes practical is genuinely right. But there's everything wrong with choosing practical out of fear, out of social pressure, out of the desire to not make anyone uncomfortable with your wanting.

The person who wants something different — something that doesn't fit the expected mold — has to be prepared to want it alone for a while. Has to be prepared to explain themselves to people who won't understand. Has to be prepared for the quiet loneliness of choosing a path nobody else chose for them.

That's the cost of wanting something that's actually yours. The cost is that you might be the only one who wants it.

---

## The Shift

Chasing the wrong thing harder is not the answer.

You can work harder at the career you inherited. You can try more aggressively to make the relationship that isn't working into something that works. You can add more hours, more effort, more optimization to the life that isn't yours. And you'll keep getting the same hollow result.

The question was never whether you tried hard enough. The question is whether you chose the thing.

Not: did you work hard for it? But: did you actually want it? Did it come from you, or did it come from somewhere else and you just absorbed it?

This is not an easy question. It requires honesty that most people avoid. Because if you admit the thing you wanted wasn't really yours, then you have to ask what you actually want. And you might not know. And it might take years to figure out. And that's uncomfortable.

But it's more uncomfortable to spend your whole life arriving at destinations you never chose and wondering why they feel like nothing.

---

## Close — The One Thing

Write down one thing you say you want. Now ask yourself: do I want this, or do I want the feeling I imagine having when I get this?

If it's the second one, go deeper. What feeling is that? What would having that feeling actually look like in my daily life? Is there a different path to that feeling that doesn't require the thing?

Sometimes the thing is just a symbol for something you could get in other ways. Sometimes the shortcut to clarity is asking what you actually want the thing to do for you.
