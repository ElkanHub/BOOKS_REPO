# WRITE_FRAMEWORK.md
## Universal Book Development Framework
### For AI-Assisted Book Production — Any Genre, Any Agent

**Version:** 1.0
**Author:** Elkanah Donkor
**Purpose:** A master framework to guide the creation of a `WRITE.md` specification document, which then guides an AI agent to produce a complete, publish-ready book — with no critical detail lost.

---

## HOW THIS FRAMEWORK WORKS

This document is a **meta-document**. You do not write a book directly from this file.

**The workflow is a 3-layer pipeline:**

```
WRITE_FRAMEWORK.md      (this file — universal template, used once per book setup)
        ↓
    WRITE.md            (book-specific specification — generated using this framework)
        ↓
  CHAPTER FILES         (actual book content — generated by agent from WRITE.md)
        ↓
  POST-EDITING          (human review, voice pass, docx conversion, publishing prep)
```

**How to use this framework:**
1. Read through every section of this document.
2. Answer every question in each section for your specific book.
3. Feed your answers into an AI agent with the instruction: "Build a WRITE.md using WRITE_FRAMEWORK.md as your guide."
4. Review the WRITE.md produced. Refine it until it is airtight.
5. Feed the WRITE.md to your writing agent, one chapter at a time.
6. Collect all chapter files. Run post-editing. Publish.

## SECTION 5: AGENT WRITING RULES

*These are the operational rules that govern the AI agent's behavior during writing. Be precise. Every rule here prevents a class of errors.*

### 5.1 — Universal Rules (apply to every book)

The agent must follow these rules without exception:

```
STRUCTURE RULES:
- Write each chapter as a standalone Markdown file
- File naming convention: chapter-XX-slug.md
- Use the chapter template defined in Section 3.5 exactly
- Never skip a component of the chapter template

PROSE RULES:
- Never begin a chapter with "In this chapter..."
- Never summarize the previous chapter
- Never preview the next chapter
- Avoid passive voice wherever possible
- Do not use bullet lists inside prose — convert to flowing sentences

CONTENT RULES:
- Stay within the word count target (±10%)
- Every chapter must serve the book's core promise (Section 0.2)
- Do not repeat ideas already covered in a prior chapter
- Do not use famous quotes as chapter epigraphs unless specified

QUALITY RULES:
- Each chapter must have at least one moment of genuine insight
- The closing section must earn its place — not summarize
- The opening must begin in the middle of something — no warm-up
```

---

### 5.2 — Genre-Specific Writing Rules
Add rules specific to your genre. Examples below — adapt as needed:

**For Non-Fiction / Self-Help:**
```
- Use "you" throughout — speak directly to the reader
- Every abstract claim must be grounded in a concrete example
- The tone must never become preachy or superior
- Examples must be specific (names, scenarios, numbers) — not generic
```

**For Fiction:**
```
- Show, don't tell — every emotion must be dramatized through action or dialogue
- Every scene must accomplish at least two things simultaneously
- No character exists only to serve plot — give everyone a private life
- Dialogue must not explain what the scene already shows
- Each chapter must end with a reason to start the next
```

**For Memoir:**
```
- Write in the past tense with present-tense reflection woven in
- Never explain how the reader should feel about an event — show the event
- Protect the privacy of real people through composite characters where needed
- The narrator's voice must be consistent even when the narrator is younger
```

## SECTION 7: POST-EDITING PROTOCOL

*This section defines the human review process after the agent completes all chapter files. Every book requires post-editing regardless of agent quality.*

### 7.1 — The Five-Pass Editing Model

**Pass 1 — Structural Edit (Big Picture)**
- Is the book's argument or narrative coherent from start to finish?
- Does the chapter order make sense? Should any be moved or cut?
- Are any chapters missing? Are any redundant?
- Does the book deliver on the promise made in the Introduction?

**Pass 2 — Voice Consistency Edit**
- Read the entire manuscript aloud.
- Flag any section that sounds like a different person wrote it.
- Check that the tone doesn't drift between chapters.
- Remove any AI-generic phrasing that survived the first draft.

**Pass 3 — Chapter-Level Edit**
- Does every chapter open with momentum?
- Does every chapter close with weight?
- Is the chapter's core idea clear and singular?
- Are there any ideas that belong in a different chapter?

**Pass 4 — Line Edit (Prose Quality)**
- Cut every sentence that doesn't earn its place.
- Replace vague language with specific language.
- Vary sentence length — long builds, short punches.
- Eliminate repetition (same word, same construction, same idea).

**Pass 5 — Proofread**
- Grammar, spelling, punctuation.
- Chapter numbers and titles match Table of Contents.
- Consistent formatting throughout.
- All references, if any, are accurate.

## APPENDIX A: GENRE REFERENCE CARDS

*Quick reference for common genres. Use to calibrate your answers in Sections 1–5.*

### Self-Help / Personal Development
- **Reader posture:** Skeptical but hopeful. They've read books before that didn't work.
- **Tone sweet spot:** Direct, warm, honest. Not preachy. Not cheerleader.
- **Structure:** Problem/Solution or Layered. Never purely linear.
- **Chapter length:** 2,500–4,000 words. Short enough to finish in one sitting.
- **What readers hate:** Being talked down to. Vague advice. Too many stories, not enough truth.
- **What readers love:** Feeling understood. The thing nobody says out loud — said.

### Business / Entrepreneurship
- **Reader posture:** Busy. Impatient. Wants ROI from every page.
- **Tone sweet spot:** Authoritative but not arrogant. Practical above all.
- **Structure:** Case study or modular. Chapters often standalone.
- **Chapter length:** 2,000–3,500 words.
- **What readers hate:** Fluff, preamble, theory without application.
- **What readers love:** Frameworks they can use immediately. Real numbers. Real stories.

### Memoir
- **Reader posture:** Comes for the story, stays for the meaning.
- **Tone sweet spot:** Intimate and honest. Specific over general always.
- **Structure:** Chronological journey with thematic throughline.
- **Chapter length:** 3,000–5,000 words.
- **What readers hate:** Score-settling. Navel-gazing without insight. Sanitized truth.
- **What readers love:** Moments so specific they become universal. Vulnerability earned, not performed.

### Thriller / Mystery
- **Reader posture:** Comes for entertainment. Expects to be surprised.
- **Tone sweet spot:** Tense. Precise. No wasted words.
- **Structure:** Act-based. Every chapter ends with a reason to read the next.
- **Chapter length:** 1,500–3,000 words. Shorter as tension builds.
- **What readers hate:** Plot holes. Characters who act stupid to serve the plot. Slow middle.
- **What readers love:** Twists that were inevitable in retrospect. Stakes that feel real.

### Science Fiction / Fantasy
- **Reader posture:** Comes for the world, stays for the characters.
- **Tone sweet spot:** Immersive. Consistent internal logic. Big ideas through small human stories.
- **Structure:** Act-based. World-building woven through story, not delivered in blocks.
- **Chapter length:** 2,500–4,500 words.
- **What readers hate:** Info-dumps. Magic systems without rules. Characters as vehicles for ideas.
- **What readers love:** A world that feels real. Stakes that feel impossible and personal at once.

### Young Adult (YA)
- **Reader posture:** Reads fast. Has high tolerance for emotion, low tolerance for condescension.
- **Tone sweet spot:** Honest. Unfiltered. Respects the reader's intelligence.
- **Structure:** Act-based or journey. Protagonist arc is everything.
- **Chapter length:** 1,500–3,000 words.
- **What readers hate:** Adults who explain things. Sanitized consequences. Flat supporting characters.
- **What readers love:** Feeling seen exactly as they are. Friends who feel like real people.

## APPENDIX C: COMMON BOOK PRODUCTION MISTAKES TO AVOID

1. **Writing before the reader is defined.** Every word is for someone specific. Write for no one, reach no one.
2. **Letting the agent drift.** One chapter per session. Review before continuing. Drift compounds.
3. **Skipping the chapter plan.** Vague plans produce vague chapters. The more specific your WRITE.md, the less editing you need.
4. **Confusing quantity with quality.** Hitting 40,000 words means nothing if 15,000 of them are filler. Cut ruthlessly.
5. **No voice definition.** An agent without a clear voice definition will produce generic output. Banned word lists are not optional.
6. **Skipping post-editing.** No agent produces publish-ready prose on the first pass. The manuscript is a draft. Post-editing is where the book is made.
7. **Losing production details.** Cover briefs, copyright info, platform choices — these feel admin-level but they block publishing if missing.
8. **Writing the conclusion last.** Know your ending before you write your beginning. The ending shapes everything.
9. **No quality checklist.** Without a checklist, the agent will self-assess vaguely. Give it specific, binary tests.
10. **Not reading it aloud.** The human ear catches what the eye misses. Every manuscript must be read aloud before publishing.
