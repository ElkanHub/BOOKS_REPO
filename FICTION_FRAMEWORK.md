## HOW THIS FRAMEWORK WORKS

The pipeline for fiction is deeper than non-fiction because fiction has more moving parts
that must stay consistent across potentially millions of words.

```
FICTION_FRAMEWORK.md     ← you are here (universal fiction system — use once per series)
         ↓
   SERIES_BIBLE.md       ← the permanent reference document for your entire story universe
         ↓
   VOLUME_WRITE.md       ← book-specific spec for each volume (generated from the Bible)
         ↓
  CHAPTER FILES          ← actual story content, written by agent from VOLUME_WRITE.md
         ↓
  POST-EDITING           ← human review, consistency check, final polish
         ↓
   PUBLISHED VOLUME      ← one book at a time, building a series
```

**The SERIES_BIBLE.md is the most important document you will create.**
It never changes. Every volume's WRITE.md is derived from it.
The agent consults it for every chapter to stay consistent.

## THE ANATOMY OF STORIES THAT HIT LIKE GREAT ANIME

Before filling anything in, understand what separates memorable series from forgettable ones.

### What great anime-grade storytelling actually does:

**1. The plot is a trap the protagonist doesn't know they're walking into.**
The reader sees it before the character does. That gap — dramatic irony — creates unbearable tension.
Attack on Titan: we sense the world is a lie long before Eren does.

**2. Every character has something they want AND something they fear losing.**
The want drives action. The fear creates vulnerability. When the story threatens the fear,
the character becomes impossible to look away from.

**3. The world has rules — and the story breaks them deliberately.**
Rules make the world feel real. Breaking them signals that something fundamental has changed.
The break must be earned. Fullmetal Alchemist's alchemy rules exist so that violating them costs something real.

**4. Foreshadowing is planted so deep the reader doesn't see it — until the reveal.**
Then they go back and see it was always there. That moment of "it was right in front of me"
is the most satisfying experience in fiction. It requires planning the ending before the beginning.

**5. Themes are lived, not stated.**
The story never says what it's about. It shows a world where the theme is the operating
condition of reality. The reader feels the theme before they can name it.

**6. Volume endings change the rules of the story.**
Each volume should end with a revelation or event that makes the reader see everything
that came before differently — and makes the next volume feel necessary, not optional.

Keep these principles in mind as you fill in every section below.

## SECTION 3: WORLD-BUILDING ARCHITECTURE

*The world-building trap: spending 80% of your energy on the world and 20% on the story.
World-building exists to create the CONDITIONS for your story — nothing more.*

### THE GOLDEN RULE OF WORLD-BUILDING:
**Only build what the story needs. Every world detail must do at least two jobs:
create atmosphere AND create conflict, or create rules AND create stakes.**
If a world detail only does one job, cut it or find its second job.

### 4.2 — Character Roles
Assign each character a structural role in the story's architecture:

```
[ ] Protagonist — the character whose choices drive the plot
[ ] Antagonist — the force in direct opposition to the protagonist's goal
[ ] Mirror — reflects what the protagonist could become (positive or negative)
[ ] Foil — shares surface similarities but opposite core values
[ ] Mentor — holds knowledge the protagonist needs but cannot simply hand over
[ ] Catalyst — their action changes everything without being the main character
[ ] Chorus — observes and articulates what the reader is feeling
[ ] Wild card — operates outside the story's established logic
```

### 4.5 — Character Consistency Rules for the Agent

```
AGENT RULE: Every character must behave consistently with their profile.

- State what each character is hiding in this specific scene
```

Every volume must advance the series toward its ending. Map the spine:

```

  Ends with: _______________________________________________ (a revelation that reframes Vol 1)
```

### 5.3 — The Volume-Ending Revelation System
Every volume must end with something that changes the rules of the story.
Not a cliffhanger (something bad happens). A REVELATION (something true is revealed).

```
The difference:
Cliffhanger: "The character is in danger." → Creates anxiety but not meaning.
Revelation: "The character discovers they've been the enemy's tool all along."
             → Creates meaning AND reframes everything the reader thought they knew.
```

### 6.2 — Thread Tracking Rules for the Agent

```
BEFORE WRITING ANY CHAPTER, the agent must:

1. List all threads currently OPEN (introduced but unresolved)
2. Identify which open threads this chapter will touch
3. State exactly what this chapter does to each touched thread:
   - Advances it
   - Complicates it
   - Appears to resolve it (but doesn't)
   - Resolves it
4. Flag any thread that has been dormant for 3+ chapters and needs a beat
5. Never introduce a new thread in the final 20% of a volume
   unless it resolves within the same volume
```

## SECTION 7: FORESHADOWING & REVELATION SYSTEM

*The most powerful storytelling tool. The hardest to execute. The one that separates
anime that gets discussed for decades from anime forgotten after one season.*

### 7.1 — The Foreshadowing Map
Every major revelation needs foreshadowing planted before it.
The rule: plant it so subtly the reader doesn't notice — but when they look back, it's obvious.


### 7.2 — The Revelation Payoff Checklist
Before writing any revelation scene:

```
[ ] Is there foreshadowing at least 3 chapters before this revelation?
[ ] Does the revelation reframe something the reader already knows?
[ ] Does the revelation raise new questions rather than only closing old ones?
[ ] Does the revelation change what at least one character must do?
[ ] Does the revelation feel inevitable AND surprising simultaneously?
[ ] Does the revelation cost something — a belief, a relationship, a certainty?
```

If any box is unchecked — the revelation is not ready. Plant more, or rewrite it.

## SECTION 10: AGENT WRITING RULES (FICTION)

### 10.1 — Universal Fiction Rules

```
SHOW, DON'T TELL:
- Never state a character's emotion directly — dramatize it through action, dialogue, or physical sensation
- Wrong: "She was furious."
- Right: "She set the cup down so carefully it made no sound."

SCENE ENTRY:
- Every scene begins mid-action or mid-thought — never with setup
- Never begin a scene with weather unless the weather IS the scene
- Never begin a scene with a character waking up

DIALOGUE:
- Dialogue must do at least two things: convey information AND reveal character
- People do not speak in complete, grammatically correct sentences under pressure
- Subtext is mandatory — characters rarely say exactly what they mean
- Avoid dialogue tags beyond "said" and "asked" except in rare cases where action is needed

PACING:
- Short sentences accelerate. Long sentences slow the reader down and draw them into thought.
- Action scenes: short sentences, minimal description, visceral detail only
- Emotional scenes: longer sentences, internal access, sensory grounding

CONSISTENCY RULES:
- Agent must consult the Thread Registry before writing each chapter
- Agent must consult the Character Profiles before writing any character's dialogue or action
- Agent must flag any moment where a character acts against their profile
- Agent must not resolve a thread introduced in this volume across volumes without explicit instruction

BANNED MOVES:
- Coincidences that save the protagonist (earn every rescue)
- Villains who explain their plan before executing it
- Characters who forget relevant information they would logically remember
- Dreams or visions that deliver plot information
- The protagonist succeeding through luck rather than growth
```

## SECTION 13: FICTION POST-EDITING PROTOCOL

### 13.1 — The Seven-Pass Edit for Fiction

**Pass 1 — Story Logic Audit**
- Does the plot make sense? Every cause has a proportionate effect?
- Does every character's action follow from their psychology?
- Are there any coincidences that haven't been earned?
- Does the volume-ending revelation reframe the volume correctly?

**Pass 2 — Thread Consistency Check**
- Open the Thread Registry. For every thread: is it where it should be at the end of this volume?
- Are any threads introduced and forgotten?
- Are any threads resolved too easily or too early?
- Cross-reference Continuity Log against actual chapters for contradictions.

**Pass 3 — Foreshadowing Audit**
- Is every major revelation in this volume foreshadowed at least 3 times?
- Are the foreshadowing beats subtle enough that first-time readers won't catch them?
- Are the red herrings plausible enough to genuinely misdirect?

**Pass 4 — Character Voice Pass**
- Read every character's dialogue aloud.
- Could you identify the speaker without dialogue tags?
- Does every character's action in every scene align with their profile?
- Is any character acting out of profile without an explicit in-story reason?

**Pass 5 — Prose Quality Pass**
- Cut every sentence that doesn't earn its place.
- Find and eliminate all stated emotions — replace with dramatized ones.
- Vary sentence rhythm: is pacing appropriate to each scene's tension?
- Remove all filter words: "she saw," "he heard," "she felt" — go directly to the experience.

**Pass 6 — World Consistency Check**
- Every world rule applied correctly?
- Every location described consistently with the Series Bible?
- Every ability or power used within its defined limits?
- No terminology drift (same thing called different names in different chapters)?

**Pass 7 — Final Read**
- Read the whole volume in one sitting if possible.
- Does it feel like one story, not a sequence of chapters?
- Does the opening image and closing image speak to each other?
- Does the ending make the next volume feel necessary?

## APPENDIX A: THE PLOT COMPLEXITY TOOLKIT

*Techniques used in high-complexity anime and literary fiction to build plots that reward multiple readings.*

### The Dramatic Irony Engine
The reader knows something the character doesn't.
Plant it early. Sustain it long. Pay it off hard.
The longer the irony is sustained, the more satisfying the payoff.

### The False Resolution
The protagonist appears to solve the problem — then discovers they solved the wrong problem,
or that solving it created a worse one. Use at Act 2A midpoint and at false volume endings.

### The Recontextualization
A scene the reader already witnessed reads completely differently once new information arrives.
The best version: the reader was shown everything needed to figure it out — but misdirected.

### The Mirror Scene
Two characters in the same situation make different choices. This reveals both characters
more sharply than any amount of description. Use when protagonist and antagonist face the same test.

### The Cost That Compounds
A sacrifice made in Vol 1 whose full cost isn't understood until Vol 3.
The reader is present for the sacrifice. They don't understand what was truly given up
until later — and when they do, it reframes the original moment as tragedy.

### The Unreliable Frame
The narrator or POV character doesn't have full information — and neither does the reader.
Not through deception, but through the limits of what one person can know.
Reveal the frame's limits at the point of maximum impact.

### The Ticking Clock Stack
Multiple deadlines operating simultaneously at different scales.
Chapter-level: this scene, today.
Volume-level: before the end of this book.
Series-level: before the final volume's confrontation.
The reader feels all three at once in the final act.

## APPENDIX B: CHARACTER DEPTH TESTS

*Before finalizing any major character, run these tests. If the character fails, they need more work.*

```
TEST 1 — THE REMOVAL TEST
If this character is removed from the story, what breaks?
If the answer is "nothing" — the character shouldn't exist.

TEST 2 — THE CONSISTENCY TEST
In five different high-pressure scenarios, would this character act consistently?
If their behavior changes arbitrarily — the profile needs more psychological grounding.

TEST 3 — THE ANTAGONIST EMPATHY TEST
Can the reader understand — not agree with, but understand — why the antagonist
believes what they believe? If not — the antagonist is a villain, not a character.

TEST 4 — THE DIALOGUE TEST
Write 10 lines of this character's dialogue without any tags or context.
Could a reader who knows this character identify the speaker?
If not — the voice is not distinct enough.

TEST 5 — THE ARC TEST
Does this character end the series believing something fundamentally different
from what they believed at the start? Was that change earned through experience,
not told through exposition? If not — the arc hasn't been built.

TEST 6 — THE READER INVESTMENT TEST
What does the reader stand to lose if this character dies?
If the answer is vague — the character hasn't been given enough to lose.
```

## APPENDIX C: WORLD-BUILDING TRAP AVOIDANCE

*The most common world-building failures in ambitious fiction:*

**Trap 1: The Museum Tour**
World-building delivered as a guided tour rather than through story.
Fix: Every world detail must emerge from conflict or consequence, never from explanation.

**Trap 2: The Appendix Problem**
Building 200 pages of world history that never appears in the story.
Fix: Write only what the story needs. The rest is private notes for consistency, not content.

**Trap 3: The Magic That Solves Everything**
A power system without consistent costs and limits becomes a plot hole generator.
Fix: Define what the system cannot do first. Then what it can.

**Trap 4: The Convenient Rule**
A world rule that exists only to solve one specific plot problem.
Fix: Every rule must have implications beyond the scene that introduced it.

**Trap 5: The Empty Villain Faction**
An antagonist faction that is evil because the story needs them to be.
Fix: Every faction must have a legitimate grievance. The reader must understand, even if they disagree.

**Trap 6: Scale Without Grounding**
Epic world events that the reader doesn't feel because they have no personal scale.
Fix: Every epic event must land through the experience of one specific, known character.
