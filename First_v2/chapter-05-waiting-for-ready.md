# Chapter 5: "I'll Start When I'm Ready" Is a Lie
### "Readiness is a feeling that shows up after you've already done the work, not before."

---

## Opening Hook (300–400 words)

You have a folder on your computer. Or a notebook in your drawer. Or a tab open in your browser right now. It’s the "someday" project. The thing you’re going to do when you have enough money, when you have more experience, when you’ve finished that online course, or when you finally feel "ready."

Let me tell you a secret: that feeling of readiness is never coming. 

You think readiness is a threshold you cross. You think it's like a green light at a traffic intersection that tells you it's safe to go. You’re waiting for the moment when the fear disappears and the confidence arrives. 

But confidence doesn't arrive in the waiting room. It only shows up on the battlefield. 

Think about the first time you had to drive a car. You weren't "ready." You were terrified. Your palms were sweaty, your vision was narrow, and you were sure you were going to hit something. You only felt "ready" to drive after you’d already been doing it for six months. 

Starting is the only way to become ready. 

The "not ready" narrative is just an avoidance strategy wearing the costume of responsibility. It sounds smart. It sounds cautious. It sounds like you're being "careful with your future." But really, it’s just your fear of looking stupid. As long as you’re "preparing," you can't fail. You can stay in the safe, warm cocoon of potential. 

But potential doesn't pay the rent. Potential doesn't build a career. Potential is just a polite word for "nothing has happened yet." 

Every day you spend waiting for the feeling of readiness is a day you’ve lost. You’re waiting for a sign that doesn't exist. You’re waiting for a permission slip that no one is going to give you. 

The truth is that most of the people who have achieved things you admire started before they felt equipped. They started when they were broke, when they were confused, and when they didn't have a plan. They started because they realized that the cost of waiting was higher than the cost of failing.

You have to decide today: which cost are you willing to pay? The cost of an awkward start, or the cost of a life spent waiting for a light that never turns green?

## Section 1: Readiness Is Retrofitted (~600–800 words)

If you look at the history of any great company, any great book, or any great career, you’ll find a mess at the beginning. 

When Steve Jobs and Steve Wozniak started Apple in a garage, they weren't "ready." They didn't have a business plan. They didn't have a marketing department. They barely had enough money for parts. They were just two guys who decided to build something and see what happened. They retrofitted their readiness as they went along. 

We look back at successful people and we assume they knew exactly what they were doing from day one. We see the polished end product and we imagine a polished beginning. We think, "Oh, they must have felt so confident." 

No. They were just as scared as you are. They just didn't let the fear stop them from starting. 

Readiness is something you look back on, not something you feel in the moment. You look back at a project you finished and you think, "I was ready for that." But if you’d asked yourself on day one, the answer would have been "I have no idea what I'm doing." 

The "I'm not ready" lie is particularly dangerous for young adults because we've been over-schooled. In school, you *are* supposed to be ready. You study for the test, and then you take the test. You follow the syllabus. You don't get to move to the next grade until you’ve proven you’re ready. 

But life doesn't have a syllabus. There is no one to tell you when you’ve "studied enough." In the real world, the test comes first, and the lesson comes later. 

Think about your friend Nana. Nana wants to start a luxury catering business. She’s spent three years "refining her menu." She’s taken four different culinary courses. She’s bought all the expensive equipment. But whenever someone asks her for a quote, she says she’s "not quite open for business yet." 

Nana is addicted to the "not ready" narrative because it protects her from the reality of the market. As long as she’s "refining," she’s a visionary chef. The moment she takes a paying customer, she’s just someone who might burn the chicken. 

You have to be willing to burn the chicken. 

You have to be willing to be a beginner. You have to be willing to do the work badly while you're learning how to do it well. Every expert was once an amateur who decided to start before they were ready. 

If you wait until you're "ready," you'll be competing with people who started three years ago and have already made all their mistakes. They’ll be three years ahead of you because they were willing to look stupid while you were busy being "prepared." 

Knowledge is not readiness. You can read every book on swimming, but you aren't "ready" to swim until you get in the water. The water doesn't care about your research. It only cares about whether you can move your arms and legs. 

Stop thinking of readiness as a mental state. Think of it as a physical location. You only get to readiness by walking through the fire of the start.

## Section 2: The Preparation Addiction (~600–800 words)

Preparation is the most socially acceptable form of procrastination. 

We live in the age of the "expert." There is a course for everything. There is a "masterclass" for everything. There are ten thousand YouTube videos explaining how to do whatever it is you want to do. 

And so, we "prepare." We buy the books. We subscribe to the newsletters. We watch the tutorials. We take the "deep dives." 

We feel like we’re making progress. Our brains are getting hits of dopamine because we’re consuming information. We feel smarter. We feel "closer" to the goal. 

But we aren't. 

Preparation beyond a certain point is just a way to delay the discomfort of action. It’s an addiction. We become "seminar junkies" who know the theory of everything and the practice of nothing. 

I know a guy, Kofi. Kofi has wanted to start a YouTube channel for two years. He knows everything about cameras. He knows about lighting. He knows about the YouTube algorithm. He has a bookshelf full of books on storytelling and branding. 

Kofi has never uploaded a video. 

Whenever I ask him why, he says he "needs to master the editing software" or he "needs to find his unique hook." Kofi is addicted to preparation. He’s using information as a shield against the fear of being seen. He thinks that if he just gets one more piece of information, he’ll finally feel safe enough to start. 

But information doesn't create safety. Only action does. 

There is a law of diminishing returns with preparation. The first 10% of your research is incredibly valuable. It gives you the basic tools you need. The next 90% is mostly just noise. It’s "busy work" that you’re doing to avoid the real work. 

You have to learn to recognize the "preparation threshold." It’s that point where you know enough to take the next smallest step, but you’re still looking for more information. That is the point where you need to stop reading and start doing. 

Every minute you spend preparing beyond that threshold is a minute you’re stealing from your future self. 

Think about why you're really "preparing." Are you actually missing a critical piece of information? Or are you just trying to avoid the feeling of being incompetent? Most of the time, it’s the latter. You don't want to be the person who doesn't know what they're doing. 

But being the person who doesn't know what they're doing is exactly what starting requires. 

You can't learn to ride a bike by watching someone else do it. You have to get on and fall off. You have to feel the balance for yourself. No amount of preparation can replace the physical experience of the work. 

The most successful people I know are "fast starters." They learn just enough to get started, and then they learn the rest while they're in motion. They treat preparation as a "just-in-time" resource rather than a "just-in-case" resource. They don't learn things they don't need yet. They only learn what they need for the next twenty-four hours. 

This is terrifying. It means you're constantly operating at the edge of your knowledge. It means you're always a little bit confused. But that confusion is the place where growth happens. 

If you feel completely prepared, you're already too late. You’re doing something you already know how to do. And if you're doing something you already know how to do, you're not growing. 

Break your preparation addiction. Put the book down. Close the tab. Do the one thing you’ve been "preparing" for. You’ll find out more in ten minutes of doing than you would in ten months of reading.

## Section 3: What Starting Actually Requires (~600–800 words)

Starting doesn't require confidence. It doesn't require a plan. It doesn't require money. It doesn't even require a good idea. 

Starting only requires the next smallest possible move. 

We fail to start because we look at the whole mountain. We look at the 35,000 words we have to write, or the ten thousand customers we have to find, or the three years of studying we have to do. We see the final destination and we realize how far away it is. 

And so, we feel paralyzed. "I'm not ready for that!" 

But you aren't supposed to be ready for the destination. You’re only supposed to be ready for the first step. 

What is the next smallest move for you right now? 

If you want to write a book, it’s not "write the book." It’s "open a document and write one paragraph." If you want to start a business, it’s not "register the company." It’s "call one potential customer and ask them a question." If you want to get in shape, it’s not "join the gym and go five times a week." It’s "put on your shoes and walk for ten minutes." 

The "next smallest move" strategy is the only way to beat the "not ready" narrative. It’s too small to be scary. It doesn't require preparation. It only requires a choice. 

Most of the obstacles you think are in your way are imaginary. You think you need a "personal brand." You don't. You think you need a "mentor." You don't. You think you need a "clear vision." You don't. 

Visions are for people who are already standing on the mountain. When you're at the bottom, you just need to look at the ground in front of you. 

Starting is an act of defiance. It’s a way of saying "I don't care that I'm not ready. I'm doing it anyway." It’s a way of taking control of your own narrative. 

Think about your friend Kwame again. Kwame wants to be a photographer. He’s been saying this for years. He finally bought a camera, but now he says he can't start because he doesn't have a "studio" or "professional lights." 

Kwame is waiting for the environment to be perfect. But the environment is never perfect. There will always be a reason to wait. There will always be a "missing" piece. 

The people who actually become photographers don't wait for studios. They take their camera outside and they shoot whatever they see. They use the sun as their light. They use their friends as their models. They start with what they have, where they are. 

This is what starting actually looks like. it's messy. It’s low-budget. It’s occasionally embarrassing. 

But it’s real. 

And the realness of a crappy start is worth more than the perfection of a "prepared" waiting room. 

When you start, the universe starts to respond to you. You meet people. You find resources. You get feedback. You realize that the "mountain" wasn't as big as it looked from the couch. 

But none of that happens until you move. 

Starting is the "filter." It separates the people who talk from the people who do. Most of the people you see around you are talkers. They have "ideas." They have "plans." They are "getting ready." 

Don't be a talker. Be a starter. 

Do the next smallest move right now. Don't think about tomorrow. Don't think about the outcome. Just do the move. Once you've done it, the next move will be visible. 

Confidence is built in the doing. Readiness is built in the doing. Success is built in the doing. 

Stop waiting for a feeling. Start moving your feet.

## The Shift (200–300 words)

You will never be ready. 

Take a deep breath and let that sink in. You will never be ready. 

You will never be 100% sure. You will never be completely fearless. You will never have a perfect plan. You will never have enough money. 

The shift happens when you decide that "ready" isn't the goal. 

The goal is participation. The goal is engagement. The goal is to be in the arena, even if you’re the most incompetent person there. 

Once you give up on the idea of being "ready," the pressure disappears. You stop having to perform a version of yourself that is certain and confident. You can just be the person who is trying things. 

You can stop being " Nana the visionary chef" and start being "Nana who is making dinner for four people tonight." You can stop being "Kofi the YouTube star" and start being "Kofi who recorded a video on his phone." 

Readiness is a prison. It’s a way to keep you in the waiting room while your life is happening out in the hallway. 

The truth is that you are already equipped to take the *next move*. You have everything you need to start. You don't have everything you need to finish, but that doesn't matter yet. You’ll find what you need along the way. 

Ready is a myth. Done is a fact. Stop looking for the myth and start creating the facts.

---

## Close — The One Thing (100–150 words)

The only thing between you and the life you want is the decision to start before you feel "ready." 

Your brain will tell you a thousand stories about why you should wait. Each one of those stories is a lie. Your fear is using your intelligence against you, creating "logical" reasons to stay safe. 

Don't listen to the logic. Listen to the curiosity. Listen to the quiet part of you that knows you’re wasting time. 

The one thing to carry forward is this: readiness is retrofitted. You don't get the confidence until you do the work. 

Start now. Start badly. Start broke. Start confused. But for the love of everything you value, just start. 

The light is already green. You’re the only one who thinks it’s red.
