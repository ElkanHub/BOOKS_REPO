# Chapter 10: Stop Outsourcing Your Thinking
### "Your mind is either a laboratory for your own experiments or a landfill for everyone else's opinions."

---

## Opening Hook (300–400 words)

Think about the last thing you were angry about. Maybe it was a political scandal, or a controversial post on social media, or a some "expert" telling you that you're doing your life wrong. 

Where did that anger come from? Did you research the situation? Did you look at the primary sources? Did you spend an hour thinking about the different perspectives involved? 

Of course not. You saw a headline. You saw a fifteen-second video with dramatic music. You saw a tweet that shared a screenshot of another tweet. And you felt a hit of outrage. 

In that moment, you didn't think. You were *thought*. 

You were the recipient of a carefully crafted signal designed to trigger a specific response. You were a node in a network, reacting to an input that was chosen for you by an algorithm that doesn't know you, doesn't like you, and only wants your attention so it can sell you an ad. 

We live in an era of outsourced thinking. We outsource our politics to our group chats. We outsource our morals to our influencers. We outsource our career choices to the latest "top 10" list on LinkedIn. We’ve become remarkably good at echoing what we’ve heard, but remarkably bad at figuring out what we actually believe. 

Outsourcing your thinking is comfortable. It saves you the effort of having to evaluate complex information. It gives you a sense of belonging because you're saying the "right" things. It makes you feel informed without you having to actually learn anything. 

But there is a massive tax on this comfort. 

When you outsource your thinking, you lose your agency. You become a puppet of whoever is most loud, most charismatic, or most technologically sophisticated. You start living a life that is a reaction to other people's priorities. 

You’re literally giving away your mind. 

Think for yourself. It sounds so simple. But it is one of the rarest skills in the modern world. It is the ability to look at a problem, strip away the noise, and ask: "What is actually happening here?" 

It’s time to take your brain back. It’s time to stop being a passive receiver of signals and start being an active participant in your own reality.

## Section 1: How Algorithms Are Thinking for You (~600–800 words)

The algorithm is not your friend. 

It is a machine designed with one goal: keep you on the screen. It doesn't care about truth. It doesn't care about your mental health. It doesn't care about your personal growth. It only cares about "engagement." 

And what engages human beings more than anything else? Conflict. Fear. Outrage. Certainty. 

The algorithm has been watching you for years. It knows what makes you click. It knows which headlines will get you to stop scrolling. It knows which "outrage" will get you to share a post. It is feeding you a version of reality that is specifically tailored to keep you in a state of constant, low-level agitation. 

This isn't just "entertainment." This is an environmental influence. 

If you spend three hours a day being fed a specific narrative about the world, you will eventually believe that narrative is "common sense." You won't even realize you’re being influenced. You’ll think you came to these conclusions on your own. 

I know a guy, Let's call him Selassie. Selassie is twenty-four. He’s obsessed with "financial doom." He follows ten different accounts that talk about the "upcoming collapse" and how "money is a lie." Whenever you talk to him, he’s in a state of panic. He’s not investing. He’s not building a career. He’s just waiting for the world to end. 

Selassie thinks he’s "awake" and "informed." But Selassie is just a prisoner of an algorithm that has figured out that "financial doom" is his specific trigger. He’s being fed a constant stream of fear-based content because that’s what he clicks on. And every click tells the algorithm to send him more. 

His reality is a hall of mirrors. He’s not thinking; he’s just reacting to a loop he didn't even start. 

Algorithms narrowing your ability to think independently. They create "echo chambers" where you only see information that confirms what you already believe. You stop seeing the other side of an argument. You stop even realizing there *is* another side. You start to think that anyone who disagrees with you is either stupid or evil. 

This is the death of thinking. 

Thinking requires friction. It requires you to be challenged. It requires you to look at data that makes you uncomfortable. If you only see what you already like, you’re just a brain in a jar being fed electricity. 

You have to actively fight the algorithm. You have to go looking for things that disagree with you. You have to follow people who make you think, not just people who make you nod. You have to recognize when your "outrage" is being manufactured for profit. 

The next time you feel a strong emotion about something you saw on your phone, stop. Ask yourself: "How did this get into my feed? Why is it being shown to me now? What does this person want me to feel?" 

If you can't answer those questions, put the phone down. You are being manipulated. 

Your mind is a property. You wouldn't let a stranger walk into your house and start moving your furniture. Why do you let Silicon Valley engineers walk into your head and start moving your beliefs? 

Take back the deed to your own brain. Start being suspicious of anything that makes you feel "absolutely certain" without you having to do any work.

## Section 2: The Group Think Tax (~600–800 words)

The second form of outsourced thinking is "Group Think." 

Humans are social animals. We have a deep, ancient need to fit in. In our evolutionary past, being out of sync with the group meant death. If your tribe thought the "forbidden mountain" was haunted, you didn't go there. Even if you suspected it was just a mountain, you stayed quiet because your survival depended on your membership in the tribe. 

That instinct hasn't gone away. It’s just moved into your group chats, your office culture, and your social circle. 

Group Think is the quiet pressure to agree with the prevailing opinion of your circle. It’s the feeling of "not wanting to be *that* person" who asks the awkward question. It’s the way you nod along when your friends talk about how "terrible" someone is, even if you’ve never met them. 

The group think tax is the cost of your individuality. 

Every time you agree with something you privately disagree with, you are paying that tax. You are trading your integrity for social comfort. You’re telling yourself it’s "not a big deal," but it is a massive deal. 

When you repeatedly silence your own thoughts to fit in, you eventually lose the ability to have them at all. You start to forget what *you* actually think. Your internal voice gets drowned out by the noise of the crowd. 

I’ve seen this in workplaces. I’ve seen teams of smart, capable people make incredibly stupid decisions because no one wanted to be the one who questioned the manager’s "vision." Everyone saw the flaws. Everyone knew it was a bad idea. But everyone also saw that the consensus was "yes." And so, "yes" it was. 

The result was a disaster that cost millions and ruined careers. All because no one was willing to pay the social price of being "different." 

Think about your own social circle. What are the "unspoken rules" of what you can and can't say? What happens if you disagree with the group’s take on a political event or a common enemy? 

Usually, the punishment isn't an execution; it’s a quiet exclusion. People stop inviting you. They stop replying as fast. They treat you with a subtle, cold distance. 

For most young adults, this exclusion feels like death. We would rather be wrong with our friends than right by ourselves. 

But here is the discomfort you need to face: if you only think what your friends think, you are a replaceable person. You are just a mirror. You are not bringing anything new to the world. You are just repeating the same tired scripts. 

The most valuable people in any environment—whether it’s a business, a relationship, or a society—are the ones who can think for themselves. They are the ones who can see the thing that everyone else is missing. They are the ones who have the courage to say "I don't think that’s right." 

Being a "contrarian" just for the sake of it is also a form of outsourced thinking. If you just automatically disagree with everything, you're still being defined by the group. 

Independent thinking is not about being different; it’s about being *accurate*. It’s about evaluating the data yourself and coming to your own conclusion, regardless of what anyone else says. 

This requires you to have a solid center. It requires you to be okay with people being annoyed with you. It requires you to be okay with being the "odd one out" for a while. 

The group think tax is paid in the currency of your potential. If you never take a stand, you’ll never lead anything. If you never question the consensus, you’ll never innovate anything. 

You can love your people and still disagree with them. You can be a member of the group without being a slave to it. But you have to be willing to handle the friction. 

If your friends only like you because you agree with them, they don't like *you*. They like their own reflection.

## Section 3: How to Think From First Principles (~600–800 words)

So, how do you actually think for yourself? You start with First Principles. 

First-principles thinking is a way of breaking down complex problems to their basic elements and then rebuilding them from scratch. Instead of saying "We do it this way because everyone else does it this way" (reasoning by analogy), you ask "What are the fundamental truths here?" 

Think about it like this: if you want to make a cake, most people look for a recipe. They follow the instructions. They reason by analogy. "This is how you make a cake." 

A first-principles thinker asks: "What is a cake? It’s a combination of heat, moisture, fat, and air. How can I create those things using what I have?" 

The first person is a cook; the second person is a chemist. The second person can invent a new type of cake. The first person is stuck with the recipe they were given. 

Most of you are living your life according to a recipe you didn't write. 

Take a career decision. You want to be a doctor. Why? "Because it’s a stable, prestigious career." This is reasoning by analogy. You’re looking at what other people have done and copying it. 

Now, apply first principles. What are the base elements of a career you want? Maybe it’s autonomy, high income, solving hard problems, and helping people. 

Now ask: "Is being a doctor the only way to get those things? Is it the *best* way? What are the trade-offs?" 

If you break it down, you might find that being a doctor actually gives you zero autonomy for the first ten years, involves enormous debt, and only lets you help people in a very narrow, bureaucratic way. Suddenly, the "safe" path looks a lot less attractive. 

First-principles thinking is difficult because it requires you to question your assumptions about your assumptions. 

I know a woman, Efe. Efe wanted to buy an apartment in Lagos. Everyone told her it was a "great investment" because "property always goes up." That’s reasoning by analogy. 

Efe applied first principles. She looked at the cost of the mortgage. She looked at the maintenance fees. She looked at the opportunity cost of that money if вона instead put it into her business. She looked at the rental yield in that specific area. 

She realized that for her specific situation, buying that apartment was a terrible financial move. It would tie up all her capital and leave her with a massive liability. She decided to rent and put the money into her business instead. 

Her friends told her she was "throwing money away." But Efe was thinking from first principles. She wasn't following a script; she was looking at the math. 

Today, her business is thriving and she could buy five of those apartments if she wanted to. She won because she did the work of thinking instead of just accepting the "common sense" of the crowd. 

To think from first principles, you need to ask "Why?" until you reach a point where you can't go any further. 

"I need to work at a big firm." Why? "So I can get experience." Why? "So I can be more valuable to clients." Why? "So I can make more money." 

First principle: You want to be valuable to clients so you can make more money. 

Now ask: "What is the fastest way to become valuable to clients?" Is it five years at a big firm where you’re an intern getting coffee? Or is it six months of intensive self-study and taking on three small projects for free to build a portfolio? 

Suddenly, the "obvious" path isn't so obvious anymore. 

This way of thinking is slow. It’s draining. You can't do it for everything. But for the big things—your career, your relationships, your health—it is the only way to ensure you isn't living someone else's mistake. 

Question the recipes. Look for the ingredients. Rebuild the cake.

## The Shift (200–300 words)

Your mind is either your own or it belongs to whoever last influenced you. 

This is the shift. You have to realize that there is no neutral state. If you aren't actively thinking for yourself, you are being thought for. You are a vessel for other people's ideas. 

The shift happens when you decide that "I'm not sure" is a better place to be than "I agree because everyone else does." 

When you make this shift, you'll start to notice how much of your "identity" is just a collection of outsourced thoughts. You’ll feel a sense of distance from your own opinions. You’ll start to look at your beliefs with a critical eye, as if you were looking at a piece of software you were about to install. "Is this useful? Is it true? Who wrote this?" 

You’ll stop being a fan of people and start being a fan of ideas. You’ll stop caring who said it and start caring about why they said it. 

Independent thinking is a form of power. It makes you unpredictable. It makes you harder to manipulate. It makes you more valuable in a world of copies. 

It also makes you more lonely. But it’s a high-quality kind of loneliness. It’s the loneliness of the person who is finally awake in a room full of people who are talking in their sleep. 

Embrace the loneliness. It’s where your real life begins.

## Close — The One Thing (100–150 words)

Stop reading the "best of" lists and start reading the "why" lists. 

The one thing to carry forward is this: Every time you encounter a strong opinion—including your own—ask "What are the first principles underneath this?" Strip away the "shoulds," the "musts," and the "everyone knows." 

If you can't find a base truth at the bottom, the opinion is garbage. Toss it. 

Your brain is the most valuable thing you own. Stop letting people park their cars there for free. 

Do the work of thinking. It’s hard, it’s boring, and it’s occasionally painful. But it’s the only way to ensure that by the end of your life, you were actually there. 

Think for yourself. Or someone else will do it for you, and they won't have your best interests at heart.
