# Chapter 6: The Story You Tell About Your Past Is Keeping You Stuck
### "Your trauma is real, but your use of it as an excuse for staying small is a choice you make every morning."

---

## Opening Hook (300–400 words)

Think about the story you tell when someone asks "What’s your deal?" Or when you’re lying in bed at 2:00 AM wondering why you’re not further along in life. 

You probably have a well-rehearsed narrative. It’s the story of the "defining moments" that shaped you. Maybe it's the story of the father who wasn't there, or the teacher who told you that you were stupid, or the business you started at twenty-one that failed and left you with debt. Maybe it's the story of how you grew up in a neighborhood where "nobody ever makes it out."

These stories feel like facts. They feel like the physical walls of a room you’re trapped in. You say "I am the way I am because X happened to me." You use these stories to explain your fear, your lack of ambition, and your pattern of starting things and then quitting when they get hard. 

But here is the truth that is going to make you very uncomfortable: your past is not a fact. It’s a story you’ve written. 

Yes, the events happened. Yes, you were hurt. Yes, people failed you. But the *meaning* you give to those events—the labels you’ve attached to yourself as a result of them—is entirely up to you. 

Most young adults use their past as a prison. They’ve designated certain moments as "traumas" that permanently broke them. They’ve accepted labels from people who didn't even know them. They’ve decided that because they failed once, they are "a failure." 

As long as you’re holding onto a story where you’re the victim of circumstances, you never have to take responsibility for your future. It’s a very comfortable way to live. If you’re "broken," nobody can expect too much from you. If you’re "stuck because of your background," then your mediocrity isn't your fault. 

But that comfort is a slow-burning risk. It keeps you small while the world passes you by. It turns your potential into a museum of "what could have been." 

You have to decide: is your story a foundation or is it a cage? 

You can acknowledge what happened to you without letting it own you. You can look at the scars without letting them dictate where you walk next. The pen is in your hand right now. You can keep writing the same sad chapter, or you can decide that the next one is going to be written by a different version of you. 

The story is yours. Stop letting other people be the editors.

---

## Section 1: How Your Story Got Written (~600–800 words)

Your identity is built out of snapshots. 

At any given second, millions of things are happening to you. Most of them you forget. But every now and then, something happens that you decide is "important." You take a snapshot of it. You put it in a folder labeled "the reason I am like this." 

Maybe it was the time you stood up in class to answer a question and everyone laughed at you. You took a snapshot. You decided that meant "I am not a public speaker." Or maybe it was the time you tried to start a small business selling t-shirts and you lost two hundred dollars. Snapshot. Conclusion: "I’m not an entrepreneur." 

By the time you reach twenty-five, you have a whole album of these snapshots. You look at them every day to remind yourself of who you are. These snapshots have become your identity. 

But notice what you *didn't* take a snapshot of. 

You didn't take a snapshot of the hundred times people *didn't* laugh at you. You didn't take a snapshot of the times you successfully completed a task or learned a new skill. You ignored the data that didn't fit the narrative of the "broken you." 

This is called confirmation bias. We look for evidence that proves our existing stories are true. If you believe you’re "destined to be poor," you’ll ignore a hundred opportunities to find a way to make money, but you’ll focus intensely on the one time you lost your wallet. You say, "See? I knew it. That’s just my luck." 

Your story wasn't written by a neutral observer. It was written by a scared ego trying to make sense of a chaotic world. 

Think about your friend Yaa. Yaa is incredibly talented at design, but she works a dead-end job as a receptionist. Whenever you suggest she apply for a design position, she tells you the story of how she applied for one job three years ago and the interviewer was "condescending" to her. 

That one thirty-minute interview has become the boss of Yaa’s life. She’s built a whole mythology around it. She’s decided that the "design world" is against her. She’s decided that she "doesn't have what it takes." 

Yaa is protecting her story more than she’s protecting her future. She’d rather be "right" about being a victim than "wrong" about herself and actually have to try again. 

We also let other people write characters for us. Your parents might have decided you were "the sensitive one" or "the difficult one." Your friends might treat you as "the broke one" or "the one who's always late." 

You don't have to accept these roles. Most of the people who give you these labels are doing it to make *their* world easier to navigate. It’s easier to handle you if they’ve already put you in a box. 

You have to look at your "defining moments" and ask: "Is this a fact or is this a label?" 

A fact is: "I lost my job." 

A label is: "I am a failure." 

The fact is neutral. The label is a choice. Most of your pain doesn't come from the facts; it comes from the labels you’ve been carrying for ten years. It’s time to start peeling them off.

## Section 2: Why People Protect Their Pain (~600–800 words)

It sounds strange to say, but many people are addicted to their own suffering. 

Not because it feels good, but because it feels *honest*. We feel that our pain is the most real thing about us. To let go of the story of our trauma feels like betraying ourselves. We think, "If I stop being the person who was hurt by my ex-boyfriend, then who am I?" 

Pain is a powerful identity. It gives you a sense of weight. It gives you a reason to be angry. It gives you a way to bond with other people who have similar stories. 

There is a specific kind of bond that forms when two people complain about how "the system is rigged" or "men are trash" or "life is unfair." It’s an easy, low-effort connection. It requires no vulnerability and no growth. You just have to agree that the world is a terrible place and that neither of you is responsible for your own misery. 

But this shared victimhood is a dead end. 

If you use your pain as an explanation for why you’re staying still, you are effectively giving the person who hurt you a permanent seat at the table of your life. You’re letting a five-minute event from ten years ago dictate your decision-making today. 

Is that what you want? 

People also protect their pain because it’s a perfect excuse. As long as you’re "recovering" or "unhealed" or "struggling with your past," you don't have to face the terrifying possibility of your own potential. If you actually healed, you’d have to go out and do the work. You’d have to take risks. You’d have to find out if you’re actually as talented as you think you are. 

Staying "broken" is a very effective way to avoid the embarrassment of failing. 

I know a guy, Dele. Dele is thirty-four. He’s been talking about writing a screenplay for a decade. Whenever I ask how it’s going, he tells me how his parents' divorce "destroyed his ability to focus." He talks about it as if the divorce happened last week. 

Dele is using his childhood trauma as a shield. He’s wrapped himself in his pain so tightly that no one—including himself—can challenge him to be better. He’s safe. He’s comfortable. And he’s absolutely miserable. 

You have to be willing to be "better" than your story. 

This means you have to be willing to let go of the attention and sympathy that comes with being the "wounded bird." You have to be willing to be the person who is doing fine. You have to be willing to be the person who has no excuse left. 

Most young adults are terrified of having no excuse. 

But having no excuse is the only way to find out what you’re actually made of. It’s the only way to build a life that is yours. 

The story you tell about your past is a tool. If it’s not helping you build something today, it’s a bad tool. It doesn't matter how "honest" it feels. If it’s keeping you in bed, it’s a parasite. 

You have to choose: do you want to be right about how much you’ve suffered, or do you want to be free? 

Freedom requires you to stop being the protagonist in your own tragedy. It requires you to look at your past and say, "That was interesting. Now, what am I doing this afternoon?"

## Section 3: The Difference Between Acknowledging Your Past and Being Owned By It (~600–800 words)

There is a massive difference between honesty and ruminating. 

Honesty is: "My father was an alcoholic and it made me feel insecure as a child." 

Ruminating is: "My father was an alcoholic, so I will always be insecure and I can never trust anyone." 

Honesty is a description of a starting point. Ruminating is a description of a permanent condition. Most of you think you're being honest, but you're actually just ruminating. You're taking a piece of data and turning it into a life sentence. 

You are allowed to have been hurt. You are allowed to have scars. You are allowed to have days where the past feels heavy. Acceptance is a necessary part of growth. But acceptance means seeing the thing for what it is—a past event—and moving on. It doesn't mean letting the thing drive the bus. 

Think about a physical injury. If you break your leg at fifteen, you acknowledge it. You go to the doctor. You wear a cast. You do the physical therapy. You don't spend the next twenty years saying, "I can't go to the beach because I broke my leg when I was fifteen." 

You acknowledge the injury, you heal it, and you move on. 

Why don't we do this with our psychological injuries? 

We treat an emotional wound from our teenage years like it’s a permanent disability. we tell ourselves that because someone broke our heart at nineteen, we are "not capable of love." This is absolute garbage. You are exactly as capable of love as you decide to be. 

Being owned by your past looks like a constant reaction. You’re either trying to please the father who didn't love you, or you’re trying to rebel against the mother who was too controlling. In both cases, you aren't choosing your own life. You’re just reacting to theirs. 

One person’s "rebellion" is just another person’s control. If your entire life is a middle finger to your past, you’re still letting your past decide your direction. 

Becoming an adult means realizing that your parents were just flawed, confused people who were trying to figure it out, just like you are. It means realizing that the people who hurt you were often hurt themselves. This doesn't excuse what they did, but it takes the personal sting out of it. It stops being a "defining truth" about you and starts being a sad fact about them. 

Once you stop taking your past personally, it loses its power over you. 

You need to rewrite the narrative. Instead of "I was abandoned," try "I learned how to be self-reliant at an early age." Instead of "I failed at my first business," try "I got an expensive education in what not to do." 

This isn't "positive thinking" or "manifesting." This is just being accurate. Every event has multiple true interpretations. You’ve been choosing the version that keeps you small. Try choosing the version that gives you power. 

You are the only person who is currently experiencing your past. Everyone else has moved on. The person who bullied you in middle school doesn't remember your name. The teacher who told you you’d never amount to anything is retired and thinking about their garden. 

You’re the only one keeping the fire burning. You’re the one providing the fuel. 

What would happen if you just… stopped? What if you decided that your story ended at the beginning of this chapter? What if you decided that from this moment on, you are a new person who just happens to have some interesting memories? 

The past is a graveyard. You can visit it occasionally to pay your respects and learn some lessons. But you shouldn't try to live there. The air is bad and the neighbors are boring. 

Get out. The sun is up.

## The Shift (200–300 words)

Your past is not a prison. It’s a chapter in a book that you are still writing. 

The shift happens when you realize that you are the author, not the character. 

A character is trapped by the plot. If the writer says the character is miserable, the character is miserable. But you are the one holding the pen. You can write any scene you want next. 

This is terrifying because it means you have no one left to blame. If you’re miserable tomorrow, it’s not because of what happened ten years ago. It’s because of the story you chose to tell about yourself tonight. 

Clarity brings responsibility. Once you realize that your story is a choice, you have to decide if you’re brave enough to choose a better one. 

Most people aren't. They’d rather stay in the prison they know than face the wide-open spaces of a life they have to build themselves. They’d rather have a "reason" for their failure than have the possibility of success. 

Don't be most people. 

Admit that you’ve been protecting your pain. Admit that you’ve been using your background as a shield. Then put the shield down. It’s too heavy and it’s blocking your view.

---

## Close — The One Thing (100–150 words)

The one thing you need to carry forward is this: you are allowed to be happy even if you were hurt. 

You don't need to stay "broken" to prove that the things that happened to you were real. You don't need to carry the weight of your past to show that you are a "deep" person. 

Your past is a set of data points. It is not a verdict. 

Tonight, before you go to sleep, find one sentence in your personal story that starts with "I am [label] because of [past event]." 

Now pull out a mental eraser and rub it out. Replace it with: "I was [past event], and today I am choosing to be [new action]." 

The story is not over. It’s just getting interesting.
