# Chapter 7: You Don't Actually Want What You Think You Want
### "If you got everything you're currently chasing, you'd be successful, miserable, and wondering why the prize feels like a punishment."

---

## Opening Hook (300–400 words)

Think about your friend Emma. Emma is twenty-seven and she just got her "dream job" as a senior analyst. She has the salary she wanted. She has the title she wanted. She even has the corner office she once pinned to her vision board. 

But if you see Emma on a Friday night, she looks like she’s just finished a marathon through a swamp. She’s drinking a glass of wine that she doesn't even like the taste of, staring at her phone, and venting about how "exhausted" she is. She tells you she just needs a holiday. She tells you that "life is just like this in your twenties." 

Emma isn't tired because she’s working hard. Emma is tired because she’s working on the wrong things. 

The most terrifying realization a human can have is getting exactly what they wanted and finding out it’s hollow. We spend our lives climbing a ladder, focusing on each step, only to reach the top and realize the ladder was leaning against a wall we don't even like the color of. 

Most of what you think you want is not yours. It’s a borrowed desire. It’s a goal that was planted in your head by your parents, your culture, your social circle, or an algorithm that wants you to buy more stuff. You think you want the "big life," but you’ve never actually defined what that means. You’ve just accepted the default definition of success that was handed to you.

We chase these false wants because they’re easy. They’re socially acceptable. If you tell people you want a promotion, they nod and agree. If you tell them you want a bigger house, they congratulate you. But if you tell them you actually want to quit your job and build furniture in your garage, they get nervous. They tell you to "be practical." 

Practicality is often just a mask for fear. And that fear is leading you toward a life that doesn't fit you. 

You’re currently running a race for a prize you don't even value. You’re sacrificing your time, your health, and your relationships for a version of "happiness" that has been pre-packaged and sold to you. 

It’s time to stop and ask: "Is this my want, or is it theirs?"

## Section 1: Borrowed Ambitions (~600–800 words)

Desire is mimetic. This is a fancy way of saying we want what other people want. 

If you see everyone in your group chat talking about getting into real estate, within six months, you’ll suddenly feel a deep, "personal" passion for house flipping. You’ll tell yourself it’s because you want "financial freedom," but really, it’s because you want to be part of the conversation. You want to be seen as someone who is "doing big things" by the people whose opinions you value. 

We borrow ambitions like we borrow books, except we never return them. 

Think about the career paths most young adults choose. How many people do you know who genuinely love accounting? How many people genuinely love being a "growth hacker"? Some do, sure. But most are there because it was the "safe," "prestigious," or "sensible" choice. It was the ambition their parents understood. 

I know a guy, Akwasi. Akwasi is thirty-one. He spent eight years in corporate law. He was miserable from the first day. He hated the hours, he hated the people, and he hated the work. But he stayed because his father is a lawyer and his grandfather was a lawyer. He’d borrowed the ambition of his ancestors and was wearing it like a lead suit. 

When Akwasi finally quit to start a small logistics company, his family acted like he’d joined a cult. They weren't happy that he was finally enjoying his life. They were angry that he’d stopped participating in the family myth. 

Most of you are currently participating in a myth. 

You want the master’s degree not because you love the subject, but because you want the letters after your name. You want the engagement ring not because you’re ready for the commitment, but because you’re tired of being the only single person at the wedding. You want the "influence" on social media not because you have something to say, but because you want to feel important. 

These are shallow desires. They aren't fuel. They are just a way to kill time. 

If you chase borrowed ambitions, you will eventually burn out. It is physically and mentally exhausting to work for something you don't care about. You have to manufacture "motivation" every single morning. You have to use "willpower" to get out of bed. 

True desire doesn't require willpower. It’s a pull, not a push. When you’re doing something you actually want to do, you find energy you didn't know you had. You don't need a "morning routine" to get you started because you're already excited to get back to the work. 

How much of your day is spent pushing yourself? If you’re constantly "grinding" just to stay in place, it’s a sign that your ambition is not yours. 

You have to be willing to disappoint people. You have to be willing to be the person who "had so much potential" but decided to do something "weird" instead. You have to be willing to let go of the social proof that comes with a prestigious title. 

The price of a Borrowed Ambition is your own life. Every year you spend chasing their goal is a year you’ve lost. And you’re not getting a refund at the end. 

Look at your goals. Look at the things you’re currently working toward. If you achieve them, who is the first person you’re going to tell? If it’s someone you’re trying to impress, then the goal isn't yours. It belongs to them. 

Give it back. It’s too heavy for you.

## Section 2: The Test: Would You Still Want It If Nobody Knew? (~600–800 words)

We live in a world of social proof. We do things so we can say we did them. We travel so we can post the photos. We buy things so we can show them off. 

Our desires have become a form of signaling. We want people to know that we are successful, that we are cultured, that we are "better" than we were last year. 

This creates a layer of noise over our true desires. It’s hard to tell what you actually enjoy when you’re constantly thinking about how it will look on a screen. 

Here is a simple, brutal diagnostic test: Would you still want it if nobody knew? 

If you could never tell anyone about your new job, or your new car, or your new relationship, would you still want it? If nobody could see your life, what would you choose? 

Think about your friend Serwaa. Serwaa is always on "the next big thing." Last year it was yoga. This year it's "digital nomadism." She’s currently in Bali, posting photos of herself on a laptop with a coconut. She looks like she’s living the dream. 

But if Serwaa couldn't post those photos, she’d be home in three days. She hates the heat, she misses her friends, and she’s actually quite bored. She doesn't want to be a "digital nomad." She wants to be *seen* as a digital nomad. She’s chasing the signal, not the reality. 

Most of us are chasing the signal. 

You want the "manager" title. Would you still want the extra responsibility, the longer hours, and the constant stress if your email signature still said "assistant"? No? Then you don't want to be a manager. You just want the title. 

You want the expensive watch. Would you still want it if it was a generic brand that looked identical but nobody recognized the logo? No? Then you don't want a high-quality timepiece. You want to signal your wealth. 

There is nothing inherently wrong with signaling. We are social animals. Status matters. But if your *entire life* is built on signaling, you are living for an audience that doesn't even like you. 

When you strip away the audience, what’s left? 

Maybe you find out that you actually love gardening. Not because it looks "aesthetic," but because you like the feeling of dirt under your fingernails. Maybe you find out that you actually hate the big city and you’d rather live in a quiet town where nothing happens. 

These are your actual desires. They are quiet. They are personal. And they are often "boring" to everyone else. 

But they are the only things that will ever leave you feeling satisfied. 

A life built on signaling is a life of eternal hunger. You can never have enough "social proof." There will always be someone with more followers, a better job, or a more interesting life. You are a slave to the comparison game. 

A life built on true desire is a life of "enough." When you're doing something you genuinely love, you don't care if people are watching. You don't need their approval because the act itself is the reward. 

Try it with one thing this week. Do something just for yourself. Something you don't tell anyone about. Something that produces no "social proof." See how it feels. 

You might find that you’ve been ignoring the best parts of your personality because they didn't fit the "brand" you were trying to build. 

You are not a brand. You are a human being. The things you want when nobody is looking are the things that actually matter.

## Section 3: Desire Under Pressure (~600–800 words)

Peer pressure doesn't end in high school. It just gets more expensive and harder to spot. 

In your twenties, peer pressure shows up as "wisdom." It shows up in the way your friends talk about "settling down" or "securing the bag." It shows up in the quiet judgment when someone decides to do something "different." 

Think about your friend Kwame again. Kwame was the "cool" one in university. He played the guitar. He read philosophy. He didn't care about "the system." 

Now Kwame is twenty-nine. He works at a hedge fund. He wears a suit. He talks about nothing but interest rates and property taxes. If you ask him what happened to the guitar, he says he "grew up." 

Kwame didn't grow up. Kwame just folded under the pressure. 

He saw his friends getting married. He saw them buying cars. He felt the invisible weight of being "behind." And so, he traded his curiosity for a paycheck that he now uses to buy things he doesn't need to impress people he doesn't like. 

We are incredibly sensitive to the "defaults" of our social circle. If all your friends are getting engaged, you will suddenly find yourself looking at rings, even if your relationship is falling apart. If all your friends are getting promotions, you will feel like a failure if you stay in the same role, even if you love your work. 

This is the pressure of the crowd. It’s a slow-motion push toward the center. It’s a way to make everyone uniform, predictable, and safe. 

But you aren't safe. You’re just disappearing. 

You have to learn to recognize the voice of borrowed desire. It usually starts with "I should..." Or "It would be sensible to..." Or "Everyone else is..." 

Your true desires don't start with "should." They start with "I want." Or "I wonder." 

The people around you are not neutral. They have an interest in you being like them. If you stay the same, they feel comfortable. If you change, they have to question their own choices. Your success in an unconventional field is a threat to their "safe" choices. 

This is why they will try to discourage you. They aren't trying to be "mean." They’re just trying to protect their own reality. 

You need to find a way to quiet the noise. You need to spend time with people who don't care about the traditional metrics of success. You need to read books by people who took the path less traveled. You need to remind yourself that there are a thousand different ways to live a good life, and most of them don't look like an Instagram ad. 

The most dangerous pressure is the pressure to be "realistic." 

"Be realistic" is just code for "don't do anything that makes me feel insecure." It’s a way to kill big dreams before they even have a chance to breathe. People will tell you to "be realistic" because they’ve given up on their own dreams and they can't stand the thought of you succeeding where they failed. 

Listen to your own data. What do you actually enjoy? What makes you forget to check your phone? What would you do for free if you were already a billionaire? 

The answer to those questions is the only thing you should be chasing. 

Don't let the pressure of the crowd turn you into a caricature of a "successful" adult. Don't trade your uniqueness for a set of social proof markers. 

The center is a very crowded, very boring place. Get out to the edges. That’s where the light is.

## The Shift (200–300 words)

Chasing the wrong thing harder won't fix the problem. 

This is the mistake most young adults make. They feel the hollowness of their "false wants," and they assume the solution is more. "If I just get the *next* promotion, I’ll feel better." "If I just buy a *bigger* house, I’ll be happy." 

They are like people trying to put out a fire with gasoline. 

The shift happens when you realize that the goal itself is the problem. 

You have to be willing to stop. You have to be willing to look at your "ideal life" and admit that it might be a total lie. You have to be willing to tear down the ladder you’ve been climbing and start over on the ground. 

This feels like failure. It feels like you’ve "wasted time." 

But you haven't wasted time. You’ve been gathering data. You now know what *doesn't* work. You now know what a hollow success looks like. That knowledge is more valuable than any degree or promotion. 

The only wasted life is the one spent chasing a goal you never chose. 

The shift is from "What does success look like to them?" to "What does satisfaction feel like to me?" 

Satisfaction is an internal metric. It’s a feeling of alignment. It’s the sense that you’re where you’re supposed to be, doing what you’re supposed to do. You can't fake it. You can't filter it. And you certainly can't borrow it from someone else.

---

## Close — The One Thing (100–150 words)

Your desires are the only thing that actually belong to you. Everything else—your background, your education, your reputation—is just furniture in the room of your life. 

Don't let other people choose the furniture. 

The one thing to carry forward is this: If you could never tell anyone about your success, would you still want it? 

If the answer is no, then stop chasing it. You’re building a prison for yourself and you’re proud of how high the walls are. 

Find the thing you’d do if you were the only person on earth. Then do that thing. Even if it makes people nervous. Even if it doesn't have a prestigious title. 

The world has enough successful, miserable people. What it needs is more people who actually want what they have.
