# Chapter 9: Learn to Sit With Not Knowing
### "The smartest person in the room is usually the one who is most comfortable saying 'I don't know yet.'"

---

## Opening Hook (300–400 words)

Think about your friend Araba. Araba is a "fixer." If you tell her about a problem you're having—a bad breakup, a career crisis, a health scare—she has an answer within ten seconds. She tells you exactly what to do. She tells you exactly why it happened. She tells you exactly how it’s going to end. 

Araba feels like a source of strength, doesn't she? We love people like Araba because they give us the one thing we crave more than anything else: certainty. 

But Araba is a liar. 

The world is a chaotic, unpredictable, and incredibly complex place. Most of the situations we find ourselves in involve a thousand different variables that we can't possibly control or even see. There are no easy answers. There are no "three simple steps." 

Araba’s certainty is a performance. It’s a defense mechanism. She isn't giving you an answer because she’s wise; she’s giving you an answer because she can't handle the discomfort of an open question. She would rather be wrong and certain than right and unsure. 

Most of us are like Araba. We’re addicted to certainty. We feel a physical sense of anxiety when we don't have an answer. We feel "weak" or "lost" when we have to admit that we don't know what’s going to happen. 

This addiction leads to some of the worst decisions of our lives. We commit to the wrong careers because we want to tell our parents we have a "plan." We stay in the wrong relationships because it’s better than being "uncertain" for six months. We accept the first easy explanation for our problems because looking for the real truth is too much work. 

If you want to think better, you have to learn to sit with not knowing. 

You have to learn to hold an open question in your mind without forcing an answer. You have to learn to let the anxiety be there without reacting to it. You have to realize that most of the answers you've been given are just stories we tell ourselves to feel safe in the dark. 

The most powerful mental tool you can develop is a high tolerance for uncertainty. It’s the ability to function, to build, and to lead while holding the truth that you don't have all the answers. 

This is the beginning of real honesty. It’s the beginning of a life that isn't a reaction to your own fear. It’s time to stop pretending. It’s time to let the questions breathe.

## Section 1: Why Certainty is Addictive (~600–800 words)

Certainty is a biological survival mechanism. 

Your brain hates uncertainty. In fact, studies show that people would rather receive an electric shock *now* than wait for a potentially larger shock at an uncertain time in the future. We would rather choose a known negative outcome than an unknown possibility. 

Uncertainty signals "danger." In our evolutionary past, if you didn't know what was behind the bush, your brain assumed it was a predator. If you didn't know if the fruit was poisonous, you didn't eat it. Your brain was evolved for survival, not for complexity. 

But in the modern world, the "predators" are different. They are career shifts, economic downturns, and changing social structures. Your brain is still using the same ancient hardware to manage these things. It wants a simple, "yes or no" answer to a problem that has ten different shades of gray. 

And so, we create "false certainty." 

We adopt labels. "I am a Democrat." "I am a Buddhist." "I am a person who doesn't like change." These labels give us a script. They tell us what to think about a thousand different things without us having to do the work of thinking. We feel secure because we have a group to tell us the "right" answers. 

We also look for gurus. We look for the "expert" who seems to have it all figured out. We listen to the podcast or the influencer who gives us a "simple framework" for life. We outsource our uncertainty to them. We feel better because *they* sound certain, even if what they're saying is absolute garbage. 

This addiction to certainty makes us mentally lazy. It also makes us dangerous. 

When you’re certain about something, you stop looking for more information. You stop questioning your assumptions. You start filter-focusing only on the data that proves you’re right. You become a "closed system." 

Think about your friend Kwame. Kwame is certain that "all politicians are exactly the same." No matter what evidence you show him—new policies, different outcomes, specific records—he just says the same thing. He’s found a "truth" that saves him from having to pay attention. He’s trade his intelligence for a feeling of being "right." 

But the world doesn't care about your feeling of being right. Reality is constantly moving. If you aren't able to change your mind, you are effectively blind. 

True intelligence is the ability to hold two opposing ideas in your mind at the same time and still function. It’s the ability to say "This seems true based on what I know, but I might be missing something." 

Uncertainty is the birthplace of curiosity. When you admit you don't know, your brain starts looking for answers. It starts asking questions. It starts paying attention. 

We avoid uncertainty because it feels like "losing control." But control is an illusion anyway. You can be 100% certain about your life plan today and have it fall apart by tomorrow morning. Certainty doesn't make you safe; it just makes you surprised when things go wrong. 

Tolerance for uncertainty is a muscle. You build it by voluntarily putting yourself in situations where you don't have the answers. You build it by reading things that disagree with you. You build it by sitting in a room alone and asking yourself "What if I'm wrong about everything?" 

The first time you do this, it feels like falling. Your brain will scream at you to find a "safe" thought to grab onto. It will try to pull you back into the comfort of your old labels. 

Stay in the fall. Lean into the not knowing. Once the panic subsides, you’ll find that the view is much better from here. You’ll find that you can see more, hear more, and learn more. 

You’ll stop being a person who is "always right" and start being a person who is "always learning." And in the modern world, that’s the only form of certainty that actually works.

## Section 2: The People Who Perform Certainty (~600–800 words)

In every group, there is someone who projects absolute confidence. We often mistake this for leadership. We assume that because someone sounds sure, they must know what they're doing. 

Wait. Think about the "guru" on your screen. Think about the person at the office who always has a definitive opinion on everything. Have you ever noticed that their certainty doesn't seem to correlate with their actual success? 

Performed certainty is a social tool. It’s used to win arguments, to gain followers, and to soothe the anxieties of a crowd. It’s a way of saying "I have the answer, so you don't have to worry anymore." Or more honestly, "Trust me so I can feel important." 

Most of the voices that shout the loudest are doing so because they are the most uncertain underneath. 

They use noise to cover up the silences in their own logic. They use charisma to distract you from their lack of evidence. They are performing for you, but more importantly, they are performing for themselves. They need your belief in their certainty to keep their own doubt at bay. 

We see this in every field. The "visionary" CEO who makes bold predictions that never come true. The "lifestyle expert" whose own life is a mess. The "pundit" who has been wrong about every event for the last ten years. 

Why do we keep listening to them? Because we would rather listen to a confident liar than an honest person who says "I don't know." 

Honesty is socially expensive. If you’re at a meeting and the boss asks for a solution, the person who shouts a bad idea with confidence will often be listened to more than the person who says "I need to look at the data before I can give you an answer." The second person looks "indecisive" or "weak." 

This is how terrible companies get built. This is how terrible countries are run. It’s a literal cult of certainty. 

Think about your friend Mansa. Mansa is an engineer. She is incredibly smart, but she rarely speaks up in meetings because she is painfully aware of how much she *doesn't* know. She sees all the different ways a project could fail. She sees all the hidden costs. She’s honest with her uncertainty. 

As a result, her louder, less-competent colleagues often get the promotions. They promise the world and then blame "outside factors" when it doesn't happen. By then, they’ve already moved on to the next "visionary" role. 

You have to learn to decode performed certainty. When someone sounds too sure, ask yourself: "How could they possibly know that?" Look for the evidence, not the charisma. 

One of the most valuable skills you can develop is the ability to spot a "fake expert." A real expert is someone who knows the limits of their knowledge. They will tell you exactly what they don't know. They will use words like "probability," "possibility," and "it depends." 

The fake expert will use words like "always," "never," and "it’s simple." 

The world is not simple. 

When you perform certainty for others, you are also trapping yourself. You are committing to a position that you can't easily walk back from. You are tying your ego to a specific answer. If you find out you’re wrong, you can't admit it without "losing face." 

This leads to a lifetime of defending bad decisions. You stay in the wrong career because you were so certain it was "the one." You stay in the wrong argument because you were so loud about it. 

The people who navigate life best are the ones who have a high tolerance for being wrong. They treat their ideas as hypotheses, not identities. They are constantly testing and refining. And they are the ones who are most comfortable saying "I don't know yet." 

This doesn't mean you become a push-over. It doesn't mean you never take a stand. It means you take a stand based on the best information you have, while remaining open to new information. 

It’s the difference between strength and rigidity. An oak tree is rigid; it snaps in a storm. A bamboo stalk is flexible; it bends and survives. 

Stop performing certainty. Start performing curiosity. It’s much more useful, and it attracts much more interesting people.

## Section 3: Building a Tolerance for Open Questions (~600–800 words)

An open question is an invitation. 

Most of us respond to an open question like we’ve been handed a live grenade. We want to get rid of it as fast as possible. We want to "solve" it so we can stop thinking about it. 

But some questions shouldn't be solved immediately. Some questions need to breathe. 

Think about the "big" questions in your life. "What should I do with my career?" "Is this the person I should be with?" "What kind of impact do I want to have?" 

If you force an answer to these questions at twenty-two, you’re using the brain of a twenty-two-year-old to make a decision for the rest of your life. You’re cutting off all the possibilities that you haven't even discovered yet. 

What if you just let the question stay open? 

What if you woke up every morning and acknowledged that you don't know the final answer yet? What if you decided to gather more data for the next six months before making a decision? 

This feels like procrastination. But it isn't. Procrastination is avoiding the question. Tolerance is holding the question while you live your life. 

Practical move: Write your biggest question down on a piece of paper. Not "how do I get rich?" but a real, honest question. "What am I actually curious about?" Or "Why do I feel so angry every Tuesday?" 

Now, put that paper on your desk or stick it to your wall. Don't answer it. Just look at it every day. Learn to be comfortable with the fact that the answer isn't there yet. 

See what happens inside you. At first, you’ll feel a frantic need to write something. You’ll want to put down a "nice" answer that makes you feel good. Resist the urge. Let the void be there. 

After a few days, the panic starts to fade. You start to notice things in your environment that relate to the question. You notice a conversation you had. You notice a paragraph in a book. You notice a feeling you had while you were walking. 

These are pieces of the answer. They are emerging from your life, not from your ego. 

By holding the question open, you’re allowing a deeper version of yourself—one that isn't driven by social pressure or fear—to participate in the answer. You’re giving yourself permission to be complex. 

Tolerance for open questions also means being okay with other people's confusion. When people ask you "So, what are you doing next?" and you say "I'm not sure yet, I'm exploring a few things," they will look uncomfortable. They will try to help you "fix" it. They will offer you their certainties. 

You have to be strong enough to say "Thanks, but I'm okay with the not knowing for now." 

This is incredibly hard to do in our culture. We are taught that "indecision" is a failure. We are taught that "clarity" is a virtue. But clarity that is forced is just a hallucination. 

I know a woman, Adwoa. Adwoa is thirty-four. She was a doctor for eight years and hated it. When she quit, everyone wanted to know "what's the new plan?" Adwoa didn't have one. She spent a year working in a bakery, reading books, and taking long walks. She stayed in the uncertainty. 

Her family was horrified. They thought she was "having a breakdown." But Adwoa was actually having a "breakthrough." She was stripping away the borrowed "doctor" identity so she could see what was left. 

A year later, she realized she wanted to work in public health policy—something she would never have considered while she was in the middle of her certain doctor life. She found her own answer because she was brave enough to sit with no answer for a while. 

Most of the mistakes we make are not because we didn't have an answer. They are because we had an answer too early. We committed to a path before we really understood the terrain. 

Learn to value the "wait." Learn to value the "grey area." The world is not binary. It’s not black and white. It’s a vibrating, shifting mess of possibilities. 

If you can navigate that mess without needing a map, you are truly free. You can move in any direction. you can change your mind as the data changes. You are no longer a prisoner of your own declarations. 

Stop looking for the answer. Start looking for the right question. And once you find it, have the courage to let it go unanswered for as long as it needs.

## The Shift (200–300 words)

"I don't know yet" is one of the most powerful things an honest mind can say. 

This is the shift. Most of us view "I don't know" as a sign of weakness or ignorance. We think it’s something to be ashamed of. 

But "I don't know" is the only honest starting point for any real inquiry. It is the statement of a mind that is open, curious, and humble before the complexity of reality. 

When you say "I don't know," you are effectively killing your ego. You are admitting that you aren't the center of the universe. You are admitting that your previous stories were incomplete. 

And in that death of the ego, something else is born: clarity. 

Clarity isn't the same as certainty. Certainty is a feeling. Clarity is a perception. You can be clear about the fact that you're uncertain. You can be clear about the trade-offs involved in a decision. You can be clear about the limits of your own knowledge. 

The shift is moving from a life where you need to *be* right to a life where you need to *see* things correctly. 

When you no longer need to be right, you stop being defensive. You stop reacting to criticism. You stop filtering the world to fit your biases. You start seeing reality as it actually is, not as you want it to be. 

This is the foundation of all good thinking. It starts with the silence of not knowing.

## Close — The One Thing (100–150 words)

The one thing to carry forward is this: certainty is a sedative, but uncertainty is a stimulant. 

Uncertainty is what keeps you awake. It’s what keeps you paying attention. It’s what keeps you growing. 

The next time you’re faced with a big decision or a difficult problem and you feel that frantic need to have an answer, stop. Take a breath. And say out loud: "I don't know yet." 

Notice how the air changes in the room. Notice how your body relaxes. And notice how your brain starts to actually look at the problem instead of just trying to escape the feeling. 

Don't be afraid of the dark. The dark is where the stars are most visible. 

Stay with the question. The answer will find you when it’s ready.
