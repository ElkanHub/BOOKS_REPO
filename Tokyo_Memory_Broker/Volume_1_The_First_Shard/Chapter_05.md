# Chapter 5: The Anchor Hole

Ren Sato’s safehouse wasn't a penthouse. It was a hole in the wall of a forgotten district between Shibuya and Shinjuku, a place the mag-lev lines avoided and the Loom-network barely touched. 

He didn't take the elevators. He didn't use the stairs. He climbed the fire-escape of a condemned laundromat, his fingers numb from the rain and the cold. 

Inside, the apartment smelled of burnt toast and ancient upholstery. It was small, cluttered with the detritus of a man who didn't exist officially. There were no photographs on the walls. No personal trinkets. No digital footprints.

Just a heavy, lead-lined safe bolted to the floor beneath the bed.

Ren slumped into the single chair, his coat dripping onto the linoleum. He pulled the diamond Shard from his sleeve.

The red pulse was faster now. It was the rhythm of a predatory heart. 

He set the Shard on the table and reached for a specialized "Null-Case"—a Faraday-shielded box designed to dampen the signal of high-value commodities. He placed the diamond inside and snapped the latch.

The red light flickered and died.

But the dread in Ren’s stomach didn't. 

He checked the window. Outside, the rain was a relentless grey curtain. No drones. No Easers. Not yet.

He moved to the safe and entered a twelve-digit code. The door hissed open.

Inside, lined up like soldiers in a barracks, were his "Personal Anchors." 

They were obsidian Shards, each labeled with a date and a location. *June 12, 2074 - The First Commission.* *March 23, 2079 - The Deep-Zone Contract.* 

These were the only things Ren Sato knew for certain about himself. As a Broker, he had been required to give up his formative memories—his childhood, his first love, the sound of his mother’s voice—as collateral for his license. It was the Syndicate’s way of ensuring professional detachment.

"You can't sell what you can't remember," Tanaka had told him during his induction.

So Ren had built a new life, one Shard at a time. He’d recorded his successes, his professional milestones, even his favorite meals. He’d created a biography of achievement to fill the void where a person should be.

He reached for the "Anchors" and touched the cold, dark crystals.

"You were the first, Ren."

Arisawa’s voice echoed in his mind, louder than the rain.

"If I was the first," Ren whispered, his hand shaking. "Then what are *these*?"

He pulled out the most recent Anchor—*Yesterday - Shinjuku Harvest*. He slid it into the reader built into the safe’s lid.

The playback started.

He saw the penthouse. He saw Tanaka-san. He saw the extraction. 

But it felt... wrong. 

In the memory, Ren felt his own professional pride. He felt the weight of the commission. He felt the neutrality of the priest.

But he didn't feel the phantom itch at the base of his skull. He didn't feel the taste of static in the air. 

He realized, with a sudden, sickening clarity, that the memory in the Anchor didn't match the event he’d lived. It was a "Cleaned" version. A version edited by the Loom before it was recorded.

His entire biography was a curated fiction.

Ren stood up, his heart pounding against his ribs. 

He grabbed his Spike-Knife and a high-powered thermal scanner. He moved to the bathroom, the only room with a mirror.

He stripped off his coat and shirt. His skin was pale, the scars from his spinal port glowing a faint silver. 

He turned his back to the mirror, holding the thermal scanner over his shoulder. 

He scanned his spine.

The screen on the scanner remained blue. No tracer. No foreign bodies. 

"Come on," Ren hissed. "Where is it?"

He scanned his clothes. Blue. 

He scanned his shoes. Blue. 

Then, he scanned the base of his skull.

A single, brilliant red dot appeared on the scanner’s screen. It was located exactly two inches below the bottom of his spinal port, buried deep within the synthesized vertebrate.

It wasn't a Shard. It wasn't a physical tag. 

It was a "Ghost-Thread"—a piece of bio-organic code that was rewriting his internal sensors to ignore its presence. It wasn't just broadcasting his location; it was broadcasting his internal state. His heart rate. His pupil dilation. His thoughts.

They hadn't just tagged him. They were *reading* him.

Ren gripped the edge of the sink, his knuckles white.

"You were the first," he whispered to his reflection. 

The reflection didn't answer. 

Ren reached for the Spike-Knife and a bottle of high-proof antiseptic. He looked at the red dot on the scanner, then at the mirror.

If he didn't cut it out now, Tanaka would be at his door before the rain stopped.

He didn't have a past to remember. But he was damn well going to survive to have a future.

He poured the antiseptic over the knife and his neck. Then, without a sound, he began to cut.
