# Chapter 2: Dust in Akihabara

The mag-lev hum died a mile outside the Akihabara sub-district. Here, the "Electrical Deadzone" began—a jagged scar in Neo-Tokyo’s infrastructure where the Syndicate’s Loom-network stuttered and failed. The neon dominance of Shinjuku was replaced by the flickering, sickly green of emergency lamps and the scent of ozone and rotting plastic.

Ren stepped out of the drone-cab. The AI driver, programmed with a survival instinct that bordered on cowardice, didn't wait for a tip. It banked hard and spiraled back toward the safety of the Upper Wards, leaving Ren alone with the silence.

It was a heavy silence, the kind that came from millions of forgotten machines grinding their teeth in the dark.

"Identify," a gravelly voice echoed from a pile of rusted server racks.

Ren didn't reach for his weapon. He reached for his license—the physical one, etched in silver and stamped with the Kanda seal. He held it up. The silver caught a stray beam of moonlight filtered through the smog.

A small, spider-legged security drone skittered out from the wreckage. Its optical sensor was cracked, weeping a viscous lubricant. It scanned the license, the red laser tracing the contours of the Syndicate eagle.

"Broker 4-0-9. Sato, Ren. Clearance: Platinum. Proceed to Sector 7. Warning: The air is 40% static. Deep-reading is not recommended."

Ren pocketed the license and began to walk. The ground was soft, covered in a thick layer of "Data-Dust"—the physical residue of burned-out Shards and discarded hardware. Every step released a faint, high-pitched whine, like the ghost of a scream.

Akihabara used to be the heart of the tech boom. Now, it was a cemetery for ideas. Dr. Arisawa had chosen his tomb well.

Ren checked his multi-tool. The compass needle spun aimlessly, caught in the localized magnetic storms that plagued the district. He’d have to do this the old-fashioned way: following the smell of dying circuits.

As he moved deeper into the ruins, the dread he’d felt in the cab began to solidify. It wasn't just the atmosphere; it was the *lack* of it. In Shinjuku, the air was crowded with the emotional runoff of ten million souls—the static of greed, the hum of lust, the low throb of ambient anxiety. Here, there was nothing. No echoes. No residue. Just the raw, mathematical void of a place that had been deleted from the Loom’s memory.

He turned a corner into a narrow alley and stopped.

Ahead, the skeleton of an old department store leaned precariously against a residential tower. At its base, a small fire flickered—not orange, but a pale, synthetic blue. 

Around the fire sat three figures. They were "The Hollowed"—donors who had given too much and been left with nothing but the hunger for more. They didn't look up as Ren approached. Their eyes were fixed on the blue flames, their expressions frozen in the slack-jawed bliss of shared hallucinations.

Ren stepped around them, careful not to break their circle. To disturb a Hollowed in mid-experience was to invite a psychotic break that usually ended in blood.

He found the entrance to Dr. Arisawa’s facility behind a stack of shipping containers. It was a pressure-sealed door, the kind used in deep-sea labs. Ren pressed his palm to the sensor.

The door hissed open, releasing a cloud of cold, chemical vapor.

The interior was a mess of analog gear—reel-to-reel tapes, cathode-ray monitors, and hand-soldered boards. It was technology from a century ago, shielded against the Syndicate’s Pulse-Waves.

In the center of the room, suspended in a life-support cradle, was a man who looked less like a human and more like a collection of frayed wires and translucent skin.

"Arisawa," Ren whispered.

The man’s eyes flickered open. They were cloudy, milky with cataracts and the white-noise of a thousand experimental extractions.

"Is that... you, Sato?" the doctor rasped. "I thought you'd be... taller. In my memories, you always stood above the world."

Ren moved closer, his hand hovering over the Loom-needle in his pocket. The professional urge to harvest was a physical ache, a secondary heartbeat. "You sent the alert. Code First-Shard. Why now, Arisawa? Why crawl out of the deadzone after ten years?"

Arisawa coughed, a wet, rattling sound. "Because the Loom is hungry, Ren. It’s started to eat its own architects. Tanaka is cleaning house. He’s deleting the past to make room for a future where nobody has to remember what we did."

He reached out a trembling hand, grabbing Ren’s sleeve. "The Shard I have... it’s not a memory of a life. It’s a memory of a *death*. A death that never happened. Or rather, a death that was undone."

Ren felt a chill crawl up his spine. "I don't deal in theology, Doctor. I deal in Shards."

"Then harvest me," Arisawa said, his voice suddenly sharp. "Before the drones find the signal. They're coming, Ren. Not for me. They're coming for the Shard you're about to take."

As if on cue, the ceiling above them groaned. The low, ominous thrum of Syndicate-hacked 'Ripper' drones filled the air, the sound of a hundred spinning blades descending through the static.

Ren looked at the dying man, then at the obsidian Shard waiting on the console. 

"Don't let them delete you again," Arisawa whispered.

The first Ripper drone smashed through the skylight, its red eye locking onto Ren.
