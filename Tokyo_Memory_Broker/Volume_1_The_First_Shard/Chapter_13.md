# Chapter 13: The Burning Anchor

The smell of ozone and burning insulation hit Ren before he even reached the third-floor landing of the safehouse. It wasn't the scent of a routine fire; it was the sharp, chemical reek of a "Pulse-Purge." 

Ren skidded to a halt, his hand going to the Spike-Knife. Beside him, Hana pressed her back against the peeling wallpaper, her Siphon pulsing a frantic, rhythmic blue. 

"They're inside," she whispered. 

Ren didn't say anything. He didn't have to. The sound of high-frequency resonance staves humming from behind his apartment door was enough. 

*Thump. Thump. Thump.*

The Syndicate didn't knock. They erased. 

Ren kicked the door open. 

The apartment was a disaster of white-noise and molten hardware. The lead-lined safe beneath the bed had been torn open, the metal glowing a dull, angry red from a targeted laser-burn. 

In the center of the room stood three Easers, their matte-black suits reflecting the flickering blue light of the Pulse-Purge. One of them was holding a mesh bag filled with obsidian crystals. 

Ren’s Personal Anchors. 

"Drop it," Ren said, his voice a low, dangerous rasp. 

The Easer didn't drop the bag. He held it over the "Purge-Vat"—a portable containment unit designed to dissolve Shard-data in seconds. 

"Broker 4-0-9," the Easer’s voice echoed through his helmet, cold and devoid of human inflection. "Your license has been revoked. Your assets are now the property of the Kanda Syndicate. Proceed to Calibration for immediate reset." 

Ren lunged. 

He didn't use a gun. He used the momentum of his own synth-bio body, slamming into the lead Easer with the force of a mag-lev collision. The Spike-Knife flashed, cutting through the Easer’s magnetic shielding and burying itself in the suit’s power-cell. 

The Easer spasmed, blue sparks geysering from his chest. 

Hana followed, her Siphon singing as she unleashed a targeted pulse that scrambled the optics of the other two Easers. She moved with a fluid, animal grace, her movements a blur of flight-jacket and focused rage. 

But it wasn't enough. 

The Easer with the bag didn't fight back. He smiled—or at least the rhythmic blinking of his helmet’s sensor suggested a smile—and dropped the bag into the Purge-Vat. 

"No!" Ren roared. 

He reached for the vat, the heat of the dissolving data singeing the hair on his arms. He could see the obsidian Shards turning to liquid light, his entire biography melting into a grey, meaningless slurry. 

The memory of his first commission. Gone. 
The record of his favorite meal in Shinjuku. Gone. 
The only evidence that he had ever existed before this moment. Deleted. 

A sharp, cold sensation hit his ear. His comm-link—the one he’d thought he’d disabled—buzzed into life. 

"The past was always a temporary loan, Ren," Tanaka’s voice smoothed into his brain, clear as a bell amidst the chaos. "A Broker shouldn't be so attached to his inventory. It makes for... inefficient trading." 

Ren slumped to his knees as the final Shard dissolved. The room felt suddenly empty, the silence heavier than the static of the deadzone. 

He wasn't a Director. He wasn't a Broker. 

He was a man without a mirror. 

Hana stood over the third Easer, her Siphon glowing a dull, exhausted red. She looked at Ren, then at the smoking vat. 

"They're just Shards, Ren," she said, her voice sounding surprisingly gentle. "They weren't your life. They were just... data." 

"They were all I had," Ren whispered, his eyes fixed on the grey slurry. 

"You have the Diamond Shard," Hana said, pointing to the hidden compartment in his sleeve. "And you have me. That's more history than anyone else in this city owns right now." 

Ren looked up. The red lights of a dozen Ripper drones were already reflecting off the rain on the window. 

"The safehouse is burned, Sato," Tanaka’s voice continued. "And the Hollowed won't protect you for long. You're a ghost hunting a shadow. Eventually, the sun comes up." 

Ren stood up, his face a mask of cold, focused determination. He didn't reach for the slurried remains of his past. He reached for the Spike-Knife. 

"The sun hasn't come up in Neo-Tokyo for fifty years, Tanaka," Ren said, his voice sounding like a promise. 

He grabbed Hana and dove through the fire-escape. 

Behind them, the apartment exploded in a final, violent burst of Syndicate-ordained static. 

Ren didn't look back. He didn't have to. The anchors were gone, but the chain was still in his hand.
