# Chapter 1: The Taste of Static

The rain in Shinjuku didn’t just fall; it dissolved. It came down in a fine, grey mist that tasted of ozone and old data, clinging to the neon signs of the Kabukicho district until the kanji bled into the pavement.

Ren Sato adjusted the collar of his charcoal overcoat. He stood on the balcony of a fortieth-floor penthouse, watching the mag-lev trains streak through the skyscrapers like glowing veins. Behind him, inside the climate-controlled silence of the apartment, a man was preparing to lose his soul. Or at least, the parts of it that still mattered to the market.

"Is it... is it ready?"

The donor’s voice was thin, like paper being torn. He was a mid-level analyst for one of the smaller Kanda subsidiaries, a man whose face was a map of exhaustion and recent, crushing grief.

Ren turned away from the city. "The Loom is stabilized, Mr. Tanaka. The needle is primed."

He walked toward the sleek, surgical-grade chair where the man sat. On the side-table lay a small, obsidian tube—a Kiroku Shard. It was empty now, a hollow prism waiting for the light of a human life.

"You’re sure you want to go through with this?" Ren asked. It was a formality, a legal requirement mandated by the Syndicate, but he said it with the practiced neutrality of a priest reciting a funeral rite. "The extraction is permanent. Once the Shard is sealed, the memory of your daughter’s accident will be gone. You will remember she is dead, yes. But you will not remember the *feeling* of it. The sound of the screeching tires. The heat of the asphalt. It will be a line in a ledger, nothing more."

Tanaka’s hands shook. "I can’t... I can't sleep, Sato-san. Every time I close my eyes, I’m back there. In the rain. Seeing her doll on the road." He looked up, his eyes wet and desperate. "Is it true what they say? That the wealthy pay for this? That someone will *want* to feel this?"

Ren reached into his inner pocket and pulled out the Loom-needle. It was a needle-thin probe, pulsating with a soft, bioluminescent blue. 

"High-end trauma is the top-tier commodity in the Memory Lounges, Mr. Tanaka," Ren said, his voice dropping an octave. "There are people in the Upper Wards who have everything—wealth, health, immortality. But they have no weight. They are ghosts in gilded cages. They buy your grief because it’s the only thing that makes them feel real. To them, your tragedy is a five-star meal."

He didn't mention the commission he’d make on a Shard this pure. A "Fresh Trauma" harvest, extracted within seventy-two hours of the event, was worth more than a decade of the man's salary.

"Lean forward," Ren commanded.

Tanaka obeyed, his spine curving. Ren located the port—a small, silver-ringed indentation at the base of the skull, standard-issue for all Syndicate employees. He slid the Loom-needle in.

There was no scream, only a sharp intake of breath. The bioluminescence in the needle turned from blue to a deep, agonizing violet. On the obsidian Shard, data began to flow—not as code, but as a swirling, liquid light. 

Ren watched the Shard fill. He could almost taste the static in the air, the phantom scent of burnt rubber and rain-slicked asphalt that bled out from the extraction process. It was a professional hazard; brokers often picked up 'ghost-residue' if they weren't careful.

After thirty seconds, the violet light faded. The Shard turned solid, a dark violet crystal that hummed with a low, mournful frequency.

Ren pulled the needle out. Tanaka slumped forward, his breath hitching. He stayed like that for a long moment, then slowly straightened his back. 

He looked at Ren. The desperation was gone. The wetness in his eyes had dried, replaced by a hollow, flickering confusion.

"I... I’m sorry," Tanaka said, his voice sounding strangely light. "Why are you here, Sato-san? Did I miss a meeting?"

Ren tucked the Shard into its protective case. "Just a routine wellness check, Tanaka-san. You were feeling a bit overwhelmed. You should get some rest. Use the company-mandated sedative tonight."

"Ah. Yes. Of course." Tanaka stood up, looking around the apartment as if seeing it for the first time. "I feel... remarkably clear. Strange. I felt like I had something important to tell someone. But I suppose it couldn't have been that important if I’ve forgotten it."

Ren walked to the door. "Most things aren't."

He left the apartment and stepped into the hallway. The silence was absolute until he reached the elevator. Inside, he pulled the Shard from his pocket. It felt warm against his palm. 

His phone buzzed. An encrypted alert from Akihabara.

*HIGH-PRIORITY HARVEST. SECTOR 7. DR. ARISAWA. STATUS: TERMINAL. REQUEST CODE: FIRST-SHARD.*

Ren frowned. Dr. Arisawa was a name he hadn’t heard in years—a name that lived in the "dead zones" of his own encrypted files. 

The elevator doors opened onto the lobby. The rain was harder now, a deluge that threatened to wash the neon right off the buildings. Ren hailed a drone-cab.

"Akihabara," he told the AI. "Manual route. Avoid the Syndicate checkpoints."

As the cab lifted off, Ren looked at the violet Shard in his hand. He had sold a thousand lives, one memory at a time. He was the best in Neo-Tokyo. 

But as he stared into the dark crystal, he felt a strange, chilling sensation at the base of his own skull. A phantom itch. A memory of a memory of a memory.

"First Shard," he whispered to the rain. "What have you done to me, Arisawa?"
