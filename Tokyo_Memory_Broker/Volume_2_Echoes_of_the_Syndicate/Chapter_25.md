# Chapter 25: The Hall of Mirrors

The "Hall of Mirrors" was a cathedral of clinical white and a smell of synthetic jasmine and sterilized gold. It sat at the very heart of the Kanda Tower, a room encased in a single, massive block of lead-glass that reflected the flickering, rhythmic red of the city’s emergency lights. 

Ren Sato stood in the center of the hall, his charcoal suit turned a deep, matte-black. His hand was on his weapon, his spinal port geysering sparks and blue electricity into the dark. 

He didn't have a pulse. He didn't have a breath. He was the "Master-Loom," but he was a god of rubble. 

Around him, a dozen "Architect-Templates" were standing in the shadows. They weren't soldiers. They were Ren Sato. 

Each one was wearing a tailored charcoal suit—the same suit Ren was wearing. Each one had the sharp, intelligent face and the lopsided smile of the Director. 

"The promotion is complete, Sato-san," Kyo’s voice smoothed into his brain—not a recording, but a live, direct neural-link. 

"The city is waiting for its 'Asset-Archivist.' Why are you still fighting for a history that doesn't exist?" 

Kyo stepped out from the shadow of a server rack. He didn't raise his resonance stave. He raised his hand. 

In his hand was a small, tattered print—a physical photo, identical to the one Hana had. 

"September 12, 2084," Kyo laughed, a wet, rattling sound that geysered sparks and high-frequency white-noise from his own spinal port. 

"Yes," Tanaka’s voice smoothed from the ruins of a nearby reader. "Because today is the day the future begins. Today is the day the Loom’s 'Perfect Symmetry' becomes the only timeline." 

Ren looked at the clones. He felt the weight of the city’s gaze. He could feel the thousands of eyes in the Grey Zone, all staring at him—at the man who had just shown them their own cost. 

"I remember what the 'Recorded Future' says," Ren whispered, his voice a dry rasp. 

"It says I’m the one who deletes you," Ren smiled, his teeth a brilliant, clinical white. 

"Yield,佐藤さん," Tanaka’s final whisper smoothed into the silence. 

The clones lunged. 

They didn't use guns. They used the momentum of their own tech-grid, their hands geysering sparks and blue electricity into the room. 

Ren Sato didn't yield. He "Synced." 

He moved with a fluid, mechanical grace that felt more like the Director’s than his own. He was fighting himself, literally. He saw his own lopsided smile on every face. He felt his own "Primal-Code" in every data-pulse. 

But he wasn't looking for a savior. He was looking for a ghost. 

Suddenly, a sharp, cold sensation hit Ren’s ear. His comm-link buzzed into life—not the Syndicate channel, but a private frequency he didn't recognize. 

"Wait," Ren whispered. 

He stopped. He didn't raise his Spike-Knife. He raised his hand. 

In his hand was a small, tattered print—a photo of a young girl with a lopsided smile. 

Hana. 

It wasn't a "Hero" memory. It wasn't an "Archive" Shard. It was a "Black-Project" Shard—the memory of Dr. Arisawa’s daughter before the harvest. 

"The Memory of the Daughter," Ren whispered, his eyes fixing on the clones. 

"You all remember her, don't you?" 

The clones stopped. Their hands geysered sparks and high-frequency white-noise. Their eyes rolled back in their heads, their spines flickering with a pale, ghostly light. 

"The Memory of the Daughter is a lie!" Kyo cried, his hand geysering sparks and blue electricity from his resonance stave. 

"No," Ren said, his voice sounding like a promise. 

"It's the only thing that's real. The only thing the Syndicate couldn't 'Rewrite'." 

He slammed the Shard into the master-control reader. 

Suddenly, the Hall of Mirrors turned from white to a blinding, crystalline static. 

The image of the daughter didn't just appear on the screen. It appeared in the "Hero-Vaults" of the clones. It appeared in the "Personal Anchors" of the donors. It appeared in the minds of the people who were currently standing in the Kanda Tower’s Processing Ward. 

"I remember what the 'Recorded Future' says," Ren whispered, his hand going to the siler-ringed port at the base of his own skull. 

"It says I’m the one who saves the city." 

Outside, the first thunder-clap of the season hit Neo-Tokyo, and for a heartbeat, the rain tasted of ozone and old, unedited data. 

Hana didn't yielding. 

She grabbed Ren’s hand. 

"Let's go save the boy," she whispered. 

The world turned from white to a blinding, crystalline static. 

The Hall of Mirrors was over. The Resolve had begun.
